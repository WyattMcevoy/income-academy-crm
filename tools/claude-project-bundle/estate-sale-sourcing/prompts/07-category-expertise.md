# Prompts — Category Expertise

Use these to deepen your knowledge in a specific high-ROI category so you can spot deals other resellers miss.

## ES-60: Become an expert in [CATEGORY] in one hour

```
I want to become competent enough in [CATEGORY — e.g., "vintage
Pyrex", "Stanley hand planes", "mid-century Scandinavian teak
furniture"] to spot high-value pieces at estate sales. I have
roughly 1 hour to learn today.

Create a focused learning plan covering:
1. The "must-know" 10-15 items in this category (the ones that
   consistently sell for $100+ and that the average estate sale
   runner might price at $20)
2. The top 5 visual identifiers (markings, patterns, era details)
   I should be able to recognize on sight
3. The top 3 common reproduction / fake concerns
4. The 2-3 best resources to bookmark for ongoing research
5. What vocabulary to use when searching sold listings and talking
   to collectors

Prioritize pieces that appear at estate sales frequently. I don't
need to know about ultra-rare museum pieces — I need to know the
stuff that WILL show up in my sourcing rotation.
```

## ES-61: Research a specific rare-ish maker

```
I've seen the maker "[MAKER NAME]" a few times at estate sales but
don't know much about them. Give me a crash course:
- Who they are / were (years active, where based)
- Their most sought-after product categories
- Signature markings to look for (impressed marks, paper labels,
  stamps — with descriptions of what each looks like)
- Approximate price range for common pieces vs. rare pieces
- Any known reproduction / counterfeit issues
- Where serious collectors typically sell this maker (eBay?
  specialty auction house? dedicated forum?)

If this maker is truly obscure or my spelling might be off, suggest
alternate names I might be looking at.
```

## ES-62: Build a "walk-in checklist" for a category

```
I'm about to start sourcing a new category: [CATEGORY]. I want a
simple checklist I can carry on my phone for the first 20 estate
sales so I can train my eye faster.

Create a checklist:
- 10 item types to look for first when entering a sale
- What to check physically for each (in one sentence)
- "Immediate buy" thresholds (if priced under $X, just grab it)
- "Take a photo and research" thresholds (if priced $X-Y, consult
  sold listings before committing)
- "Walk away" red flags that save me from bad buys

Format as a single-page document I can print or keep open on my
phone screen while sourcing.
```

## ES-63: Sold-listing analysis for a category

```
I'm trying to understand the current market for [CATEGORY] on eBay.
Walk me through how to do a proper sold-listings analysis:
1. How to filter eBay sold listings to get a useful baseline
2. What to ignore (obvious outliers, misspelled listings, wrong
   categorizations)
3. How to interpret the actual range (ignore top 10% and bottom 10%
   for a typical-item estimate)
4. How to factor in condition differences between my item and the
   sold comps
5. How to factor in "listing quality" — a mediocre title with bad
   photos often sells for less than the same item well-presented

Give me a worked example using [EXAMPLE ITEM] if possible.
```

## ES-64: Seasonal patterns in a category

```
I focus on [CATEGORY]. Are there seasonal patterns in when items in
this category sell best on eBay / where I should time listings?

For each month of the year, tell me:
- Relative demand (high / medium / low) for [CATEGORY]
- Why (Christmas gifting? spring cleaning? back-to-school?)
- Specific items within [CATEGORY] that peak in that month
- Whether I should LIST or HOLD inventory during that month

Goal: time my listings for when buyers are shopping hardest, hold
inventory through low-demand months.
```
