# Honest Affiliate Marketing Starter — Full Curriculum

**Total runtime target**: 4-6 hours of video across 8 modules + intro + conclusion.
**Format**: talking head for teaching, screen recordings for tool demos.
**Tone**: honest, sober, no hype. Treat the learner like an intelligent adult who's been lied to before.

---

## Module 0 — Welcome + Reality Check (20 min)

**Goal**: reset expectations. Most affiliate courses sell a fantasy. Ours sells a real business with a real learning curve.

**Cover**:
- What affiliate marketing actually is (passing through commerce, earning commissions for referrals)
- What it is NOT (a magic button, a loophole, an overnight income stream)
- Realistic timelines: 6-12 months to first meaningful commissions, 12-24 months to significant income
- The earnings disclaimer — said plainly, once, early
- Who succeeds (patient, research-oriented, comfortable with slow compounding)
- Who doesn't (people looking for shortcuts)

**Key talking points**:
- "You're going to hear a lot of noise about affiliate marketing. Most of it is nonsense. Some of it is fraud. The real version is: you build something useful, people trust it, you recommend products, you get paid. Period."
- "If you quit your job expecting this to replace your income in 90 days, you will be disappointed and broke. If you treat it like a real small business that compounds over 2-3 years, you'll be fine."

**Homework**: Write a 1-paragraph "why" statement. Why are you actually doing this? Tape it above your desk.

## Module 2 — The 3 Honest Paths (45 min)

**Goal**: help them choose ONE path before they try all three and fail at all three.

**Cover** — with honest pros/cons for each:

### 2.1 Content Site / Blog
- What it is: write helpful articles on a topic, recommend products within them, earn commissions when readers buy
- Strengths: compounds over time, works while you sleep (literally — in the sense Google sends traffic 24/7), no face on camera
- Weaknesses: SEO is harder than it used to be (post-HCU), slow to see results (6-12 months), requires consistency
- Time to first $: 6-12 months typical
- Time to $1,000/mo: 12-24 months typical
- Best fit: patient writers, researchers, organized people

### 2.2 YouTube Channel
- What it is: make videos on a topic, mention/review products, link to them in descriptions
- Strengths: visual trust builds faster, YouTube algorithm is friendlier to niche creators than Google SEO
- Weaknesses: requires being on camera (dealbreaker for some), equipment/editing learning curve, consistency pressure
- Time to first $: 3-9 months typical
- Time to $1,000/mo: 9-18 months typical
- Best fit: comfortable on camera, willing to learn editing, patient

### 2.3 Email Newsletter
- What it is: build an email list in a niche, send helpful content regularly, recommend products when relevant
- Strengths: direct relationship with readers (no algorithm risk), high trust = high conversions, manageable at any size
- Weaknesses: starting from zero subscribers, requires consistent writing, deliverability challenges
- Time to first $: 6-18 months typical
- Time to $1,000/mo: 12-24 months typical
- Best fit: good writers, willing to be patient with list growth

**The other paths we DON'T cover** (and why):
- Paid traffic to affiliate offers: high-risk, easy way to burn money
- Instagram/TikTok: trend-heavy, hard for older demographic, low commissions relative to effort
- "Email blasting": violates most affiliate terms, gets accounts banned

**Choosing between the three**:
Decision framework in video: answer 5 questions honestly, see which path maps to you.

**Homework**: Pick ONE path. Write it at the top of your notebook. Don't change it for 6 months.

## Module 3 — Picking a Niche You Can Live With for 2 Years (60 min)

**Goal**: niche selection is the #1 reason beginners fail. Get this right.

**Cover**:

### 3.1 The niche fit test
A good niche has:
- **Genuine interest** from you (you'd read about it for free)
- **Existing commercial activity** (products to sell, not just information)
- **Depth** (enough to write/film 100+ pieces about)
- **Reasonable competition** (not dominated by 10 gigantic sites)
- **Decent affiliate programs** in the space

### 3.2 The 5 niche evaluation criteria (with research walkthrough)
1. Search volume: is anyone looking for this?
2. Commercial intent: do searchers want to buy?
3. Commission potential: are there real affiliate programs?
4. Competition level: can a small site compete?
5. Your staying power: will you still care in 2 years?

### 3.3 Niche examples that still work in 2026

**Evergreen winners** (tested, durable):
- Home gardening tools and supplies
- RV and camper accessories
- Pet care (specific — "senior dog nutrition," not "pets")
- Fishing gear for specific species or regions
- Kitchen equipment for specific cooking styles (air fryer, sous vide, etc.)
- Home office and standing desk accessories
- Hobby niches (woodworking, knitting, model trains, etc.)
- Senior-specific tools (mobility aids, easy-grip kitchen tools, etc.)

**Rising niches** (still open as of 2026):
- AI tool reviews for specific professions
- Sustainable/eco product roundups
- Specialty coffee/tea gear
- Home fitness equipment for older adults
- Grandparenting tools and activities

**Saturated/declining niches** (avoid or extra hard):
- General tech reviews (dominated by huge sites)
- Best credit card guides (Bankrate owns this)
- Weight loss supplements (FTC-heavy)
- Crypto/trading anything
- "Make money online" meta-niche (yes, ironic — don't compete with the course you're buying)

### 3.4 Screen recording: niche research walkthrough
- Open Google Keyword Planner (free)
- Show search volume research for 3 candidate niches
- Show how to check affiliate programs for each
- Show how to eyeball competition (top 10 Google results — are they all massive sites?)

### 3.5 Exercise: the 3-niche bake-off
- Pick 3 candidate niches
- Run each through the 5 criteria
- Score each 1-5 per criterion
- Commit to the highest scorer for 6+ months

## Module 4 — Your First Affiliate Partner (Amazon Associates Walkthrough) (45 min)

**Goal**: get them signed up for Amazon Associates. It's the beginner's training wheels.

**Cover**:

### 4.1 Why start with Amazon
- Low barrier to entry
- Huge catalog (you can link to almost anything)
- Cookie applies to everything — referral earns you commission on any purchase in the window
- BUT: commissions are low (1-10%), approval requires a site with content

### 4.2 Amazon Associates signup walkthrough (screen recording)
- The application
- Preferred account name
- Payment method setup
- The 180-day initial requirement (3 qualifying sales in 180 days, or they kick you out)
- The "operating agreement" — key rules to follow

### 4.3 Key Amazon compliance rules
- Must disclose affiliate relationships prominently
- Must use their approved images / their tools (not scrape)
- Can't use Amazon links in email directly (they hate this — use your site as intermediary)
- Can't display prices in a stale way (they change)
- Can't buy through your own links
- Can't incentivize clicks ("click to help me out!")

### 4.4 Creating your first 10 Amazon links
- Show the SiteStripe tool
- Creating a product link
- Shortening with their tool
- Using a plugin (AAWP for WordPress) if applicable

### 4.5 Realistic Amazon income
- Months 1-3: $0-10
- Months 4-6: $5-50 (if content is live and ranking)
- Months 7-12: $30-300 (depending on niche + traffic)
- Year 2+: $300-3,000 (for a dedicated niche site with 50+ well-ranked posts)

These are typical. Some make more. Most make less. That's the honest range.

## Module 5 — Beyond Amazon — Higher-Commission Programs (45 min)

**Goal**: teach them where the real money is, with care.

**Cover**:

### 5.1 Why Amazon's 1-10% isn't the whole story
Software subscriptions pay 30-50%. Info products 40-60%. Finance products often $100-500 per acquisition. Higher commissions exist — you just have to know where to look.

### 5.2 The 5 main affiliate networks

**ShareASale**
- Large, mature network
- Lots of B2B and SMB programs
- Payments reliable
- Approval easier than some

**Impact**
- Premium brands
- Great reporting
- Harder approval but worth it

**CJ Affiliate (formerly Commission Junction)**
- Old guard, large
- Lots of mainstream brands
- Their UI is dated but inventory is good

**PartnerStack**
- Focused on SaaS
- 20-30% recurring commissions common
- Good for tech-adjacent niches

**Direct affiliate programs**
- Many brands run their own (e.g., Canva, Bluehost, ConvertKit)
- Higher commissions but more programs to manage
- Usually found via their "affiliates" or "partners" page in footer

### 5.3 The "recurring commission" gold mine
Software/SaaS programs often pay 20-40% recurring — meaning you earn every month that customer stays subscribed. 10 referrals × $20/mo × 30% = $60/mo × indefinitely. Do this 20 times and you have a meaningful income stream.

### 5.4 Vetting an affiliate program before you promote
- Cookie duration (30+ days is good)
- Payment threshold (how much do you have to earn before they pay out)
- Payment method (PayPal, check, wire — which works for you)
- Payment schedule (monthly, net-30, net-60)
- Commission structure (flat vs percentage vs tiered)
- Reputation (search "[program name] affiliate reviews" — look for payout complaints)
- Do they disclose clearly? (Bad affiliate programs hide terms.)

### 5.5 The rule of "only promote what you'd use"
- If you wouldn't buy it yourself, don't recommend it
- If it's a bad product, your readers won't trust you anymore
- Trust compounds. Bad recommendations destroy it in one shot.

### 5.6 Compliance watchouts
- Every piece of content with an affiliate link must have disclosure
- Most networks require you to display specific disclosure language
- FTC has explicit rules: disclosure must be "clear and conspicuous" — not buried in footer
- Don't mislead about your relationship with brands

## Module 6 — Creating Content That Actually Helps People (60 min)

**Goal**: teach them to write for readers, not Google. Post-HCU SEO rewards this.

**Cover**:

### 6.1 The 3 content types that work for affiliate sites

**Product reviews** (honest, detailed)
- Deep dive on ONE product
- Pros AND cons (no fake-positive only content)
- Compared to 2-3 alternatives
- Personal perspective if you have one (did you use it? would you buy it again?)
- Length: 1,500-3,000 words typical

**Roundup posts** ("Best X for Y")
- 5-10 products serving a specific need
- Short descriptions for each
- Clear criteria for ranking
- Length: 2,000-4,000 words typical

**Problem-solving guides** (tutorials, how-tos)
- Solve a specific reader problem
- Recommend relevant products in context (not forced)
- Can rank for long-tail searches
- Length: 1,000-2,500 words typical

### 6.2 The 2026 content quality bar
Post-HCU, Google ruthlessly de-ranks:
- Thin content (under 600 words)
- AI-generated fluff
- Copy-paste reviews (same info as 100 other sites)
- Missing expertise/experience signals
- No personal voice

What wins:
- Demonstrated hands-on experience (photos of you with the product, specific details only an owner would know)
- Unique perspectives (here's what I learned that the manual doesn't say)
- Helpful comparisons (not just spec sheets)
- Clear voice, not generic "SEO voice"

### 6.3 Using AI writing tools responsibly
- AI as a first-draft assistant: yes
- AI as a final-draft generator: no (Google can tell, and more importantly, readers can tell)
- Your human editing pass is 40-60% of the value
- Always fact-check AI output (hallucinations are common)

### 6.4 Writing your first 10 posts
- Pick your 10 "seed" topics
- Research each (Module 7 covers keyword research)
- Write an outline first
- First drafts can be AI-assisted
- Human edit pass
- Add your own experience/examples
- Include the right affiliate links, naturally

### 6.5 The FTC disclosure (non-negotiable)
Every post with affiliate links needs disclosure above the fold. Example:
> "This post contains affiliate links. If you purchase through these links, I may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. I only recommend products I've personally used or would use myself."

More on this in Module 8.

## Module 7 — Getting Traffic (The Slow-and-Steady Way) (60 min)

**Goal**: realistic expectations for traffic growth + the 3 channels that actually work.

**Cover**:

### 7.1 The 3 traffic channels worth learning

**Search (SEO)**
- Slowest but compounds best
- 6-12 months to initial traction
- Highest trust signal
- Most durable long-term
- Best fit for written content

**Direct/newsletter**
- Building your own email list
- Requires active subscriber acquisition
- Highly converting when done right
- Not dependent on any platform's algorithm

**YouTube search**
- Faster than Google search in many niches
- Visual trust
- Best for reviews and tutorials
- Requires video creation capacity

### 7.2 SEO basics for 2026 (post-HCU)

**Keyword research with free tools**:
- Google Keyword Planner (free with Google Ads account)
- Google autocomplete
- AnswerThePublic (free tier)
- Ahrefs/SEMrush free trials

**What to research**:
- Commercial intent keywords ("best X," "X review," "X vs Y")
- Question-based keywords ("how to X," "is X worth it")
- Long-tail specificity ("best cordless lawn mower for small yards")

**Avoiding Google penalties**:
- Don't copy content from other sites
- Don't stuff keywords unnaturally
- Don't publish thin content at volume
- Don't buy backlinks (this is a fast way to get penalized)
- Build links naturally through guest posts, genuine outreach

### 7.3 The "100 Day Content Sprint" (recommended first-year plan)

- **Month 1**: research + outline 10-15 topics, write first 5
- **Month 2-3**: publish 1-2 posts per week, focus on quality
- **Month 4-6**: 50+ posts live, start seeing initial rankings
- **Month 6-12**: optimize winners, expand depth, consistent 1-2 posts/week
- **Month 12+**: reap compounding results

### 7.4 Link building the ethical way
- Guest posts on other niche blogs
- HARO (Help A Reporter Out) responses
- Genuine networking with other creators
- Resource pages that want to link to you
- Being quoted in articles (requires being an expert, not a just-starting-out bot)

### 7.5 YouTube SEO basics
- Thumbnails matter MORE than titles (~60% of click decisions)
- Titles: include the keyword naturally
- First 30 seconds determine if they stay
- Description: links, chapters, keywords
- End screens to drive more views

### 7.6 Email list building
- Every piece of content should have an email opt-in
- Lead magnets that actually deliver (not generic PDFs)
- Welcome sequence that builds trust before you promote anything
- MailerLite free tier works up to 1,000 subscribers
- Beehiiv is newer but growing fast, also has free tier

## Module 8 — Measuring, Compliance, and Scaling Honestly (30 min)

**Goal**: close the loop with the boring-but-critical stuff that keeps you out of trouble.

**Cover**:

### 8.1 Metrics that actually matter
- Clicks on affiliate links (leading indicator)
- Conversion rate (what % of clicks become sales)
- EPC (earnings per click) — per program
- Traffic (separately: organic / referral / direct)
- Email subscribers (if newsletter path)

Metrics that don't matter at first:
- Social shares (vanity)
- Time on page (unless you're comparing variants)
- Bounce rate (often misinterpreted)

### 8.2 Tracking tools
- Google Analytics 4 (free, basic)
- Google Search Console (free, SEO focus)
- Link tracking: most affiliate networks have their own
- Spreadsheet for rolling up monthly totals by program

### 8.3 The FTC disclosure deep-dive
- Must be "clear and conspicuous"
- Must appear before the affiliate link (ideally top of article)
- Must be readable (not white text on white background)
- Must apply to specific posts (not just a footer link)
- Applies to social media too (Instagram, X, YouTube, TikTok)

**Recommended disclosure language**:

For website:
> "Disclosure: This post contains affiliate links. If you purchase through one of these links, I may earn a commission at no extra cost to you. I only recommend products I've personally tried or would be comfortable recommending to a friend."

For YouTube video description:
> "Some of the links in this description are affiliate links. If you purchase through them, I may earn a small commission at no additional cost to you."

For newsletter:
> "Some of the links in this email are affiliate links. Details at the footer."

### 8.4 Tax treatment
- Affiliate income is self-employment income
- Quarterly estimated taxes apply
- Keep receipts for business expenses (hosting, tools, software)
- Consult a CPA especially in your first year
- Forming an LLC becomes worth considering around $20-30k/year in earnings

### 8.5 Scaling without burning out
- Year 1: one site, build it well
- Year 2: expand deeply (more content, better posts) or start a second site/channel (careful — spreads attention thin)
- Year 3: hire a writer, an editor, or an assistant
- Year 4+: consider selling the site (affiliate sites can sell for 30-50× monthly earnings)

### 8.6 When to quit and when to push through
- If you're 6 months in with consistent effort and zero traffic: reassess your niche and content quality
- If you're 12 months in and earning under $100/mo despite 50+ posts: niche probably wrong
- If you're 18 months in with 500+ monthly visitors and $0: content quality issue
- If you're 2 years in and $500/mo and rising: keep going, you've cracked it
