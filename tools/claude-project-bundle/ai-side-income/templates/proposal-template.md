## How to use this template

### Customize every section
Generic proposals get skimmed. Specific proposals get signed. Replace every bracket thoughtfully — don't just find-and-replace.

### Length matters
Good proposals are 2-3 pages. Shorter feels flimsy. Longer feels like you're hiding something.

### Pricing psychology
- **Option A is your preferred path.** Put it first.
- **Option B (retainer) creates contrast.** Even if they pick A, offering B signals that you work with serious clients.
- **Never put your lowest price first.** Anchor high, then give them a cheaper option if needed.

### Timeline buffer
Whatever timeline you think, add 25% buffer. Things take longer than expected. Under-promising and over-delivering is a pricing strategy.

### What to attach
- Services agreement (for signature)
- If relevant: a 1-2 page case study or relevant sample
- Nothing else. Don't overwhelm.

### What NOT to do
- Don't attach 10 PDFs
- Don't include your origin story
- Don't use "I think" or "maybe" language — be decisive
- Don't quote a number you'll resent later

### Follow-up schedule
- **Day 2 after sending**: a brief "did this land OK?" email if no acknowledgment
- **Day 5**: offer to hop on a 15-min call
- **Day 10**: remind that the proposal expires at 14 days
- **Day 14**: let it expire gracefully

Don't beg. The proposal says what it says.

### When they push back on price
Re-scope, don't discount. If they want $X but you quoted $Y, remove $Y-$X worth of work from the scope. Same price-per-value ratio. Trains clients that your time has a real cost.

### When to walk away
If after discovery they want to pay half your rate for twice the work: walk. Politely. With a referral if possible. That client will ruin your month.
