# Rate Increase Email — Notifying Existing Clients

Use this when raising rates with existing retainer / ongoing clients. Send 60 days before new rate takes effect.

---

## Standard version (retained relationship)

```
Subject: A heads-up about my rates in [MONTH]

Hi [NAME],

A heads-up: starting [DATE — 60+ days out], my rate for new projects is going to $[NEW RATE]. For existing monthly clients like you, I'm writing in advance so you have time to plan.

Here's how that affects our agreement specifically:
- Current rate: $[CURRENT]/month for [CURRENT SCOPE]
- Rate starting [DATE]: $[NEW]/month for the same scope

That's a [X]% increase. I know that's a number on your P&L, so I want to be transparent about why:

[ONE HONEST REASON — e.g.:
- My work has deepened over the past [N] months and I'm delivering more strategic value than pure execution
- Demand has grown significantly and I want to keep working with my favorite clients
- Costs of running my practice (software, insurance, etc.) have increased meaningfully]

What I'm NOT doing:
- Charging more for less work
- Making a play for more revenue at your expense
- Surprising you with a bill jump next month

What I'd like to discuss on our next call:
- Whether the new rate makes sense for your current use of our time
- Whether scope should adjust (more, less, or different)
- Whether this is a natural moment to explore [NEW SERVICE / LEVEL / PACKAGE]

If the new rate doesn't work for your situation, I want to know early so we can find a graceful transition — whether that's scaling back scope, reducing frequency, or winding down the engagement with plenty of runway for you to find someone else.

You're not on the hook for anything until [DATE]. Current rate holds until then.

Let me know what you're thinking.

[YOUR FIRST NAME]
```

---

## Short version (strong relationship, established trust)

```
Subject: Rate update, [MONTH]

Hi [NAME],

Quick heads-up: starting [DATE — 60+ days out], my monthly rate with you goes from $[CURRENT] to $[NEW]. Same scope, same cadence.

That's a [X]% increase. Wanted to give you plenty of notice to plan or push back.

Happy to discuss on our next call — or just reply here if it's straightforward for you either way.

[YOUR FIRST NAME]
```

---

## Version for adding scope (upgrade, not just increase)

```
Subject: Thinking about how we work together in [NEXT QUARTER]

Hi [NAME],

Our engagement has evolved a lot in the last [N] months, and I want to talk about what that means going forward.

When we started, the scope was [ORIGINAL SCOPE] at $[RATE]/month.

Over the last [N] months, the work has actually included:
- [ADDED ITEM 1]
- [ADDED ITEM 2]
- [ADDED ITEM 3]

Which is great — the engagement is deeper and we're producing more value. But we've informally stretched the scope without updating the agreement.

I'd like to propose an updated arrangement starting [DATE — 30+ days out]:
- Updated scope: [HONEST LIST — what's really happening]
- Updated rate: $[NEW]/month

This reflects what we're actually doing, not just a bump.

If you'd rather return to the original scope at the original rate, that's fine — we just pause the add-ons. Or if there's a middle ground that works better for where you are right now, I'm open.

Want to hop on a 20-minute call this week to talk it through?

[YOUR FIRST NAME]
```

---

## When to send

- **60 days minimum** before the new rate takes effect (gives them time to plan, adjust, or transition)
- **Not during a crisis or hectic period** on their end (late December, during their product launch, etc.)
- **Not immediately after a complaint or rough stretch** — wait for a calm moment

## How often to raise rates

- **Rough rule**: 10-15% every 12 months for ongoing retainers
- **Big rule**: 20-30% every 18-24 months if market rates have shifted meaningfully
- **Never raise rates by surprise** — always notice period
- **Never raise rates twice in one year** without major scope change

## What to do if they push back

**"That's a big jump."**
"I hear you. Let's look at scope — is there anything we can adjust to keep total investment in the range you're comfortable with?"

**"Can you lock in the old rate for longer?"**
Sometimes OK — you can offer "6 more months at the old rate in exchange for prepayment." Rare but worth considering for loyal clients.

**"We can't afford that."**
Honest response: "I understand. Would it make sense to reduce frequency [to bi-weekly / less work per month] to fit the budget? Or should we plan a transition?"

**"We'll need to end the engagement then."**
Respect it. Offer transition support. Leave the door open: "If things change, let's talk."

## What NOT to do

- **Don't apologize** for raising rates. This is normal professional practice.
- **Don't justify at length.** One honest sentence is plenty. Over-explaining feels defensive.
- **Don't compare to competitors.** "X is charging $Y" reads as amateur.
- **Don't tie the raise to "working harder."** It's not about hours; it's about value.
- **Don't waive it for the biggest client.** That's how you end up stuck at old rates.

## Track outcomes

Every rate increase teaches you something:
- How many clients accepted without flinching? (You probably underpriced before)
- How many pushed back? (Reasonable — expect some)
- How many left? (1-2 out of 10 is normal; more than that suggests timing or magnitude was off)
- Was the increase enough to hit your income goals? Or too modest?

Keep a simple log. Use the data to guide the next one.
