# Prompts — Pricing Conversations

The hardest part of freelance bookkeeping isn't the work. It's having the money conversation. These prompts help.

## BK-40: Initial proposal for a new client

```
Draft a proposal for a new bookkeeping client. Their business:
- [BUSINESS TYPE + INDUSTRY]
- [REVENUE RANGE]
- [TRANSACTION VOLUME ESTIMATE — transactions/month]
- [# BANK ACCOUNTS / CREDIT CARDS / PAYROLL Y-N / AR Y-N / AP Y-N]
- Current state of books: [CLEAN / MESSY / NOT SET UP]

My typical pricing for their situation:
- Monthly retainer: $[X]/month (based on [Y] estimated hours at
  $[Z]/hour effective)
- One-time cleanup (if books are messy): $[X] flat

Write a proposal that includes:
1. Brief intro (why I'm a fit for their situation)
2. Proposed monthly scope (specific deliverables, not vague "we'll
   keep your books")
3. Proposed cleanup scope if needed (specific outcomes, not time-based)
4. Pricing (my retainer + optional cleanup)
5. Payment terms (monthly in advance, ACH preferred)
6. Timeline (start date, first month's deliverable date)
7. What they get that justifies the price (reports, summary narrative,
   AI-drafted questions answered, availability)

Tone: confident in my value, not apologetic. Clients respect a bookkeeper
who respects their own expertise. Don't soft-pitch.
```

## BK-41: Handle "your price is too high"

```
A prospect told me my proposed rate of $[X]/month is "higher than
they expected" or "more than they're paying a cheaper bookkeeper."

Write me a response that:
- Doesn't immediately drop my price
- Asks what they've been paying (I need data, not a counter)
- Asks what their current bookkeeper delivers (a useful question —
  cheap bookkeepers often don't deliver reports, narratives, or
  availability)
- Articulates the specific differences my price reflects
- Leaves the door open for them to either accept my price or
  continue cheaper elsewhere — either is fine

Tone: confident, not defensive. If my work is worth $X, it's worth
walking away from someone who undervalues it.

Include a line about referrals: "If my rate isn't right for you, I
might know someone in your budget range — happy to make an intro."
```

## BK-42: Raise prices on an existing client

```
I've been working with [CLIENT] for [DURATION] at $[CURRENT RATE]/mo.
Their scope has grown [or] market rates have increased [or] I need
to raise prices to match my value.

Write me an email announcing a rate increase:
- Timing: effective [DATE — typically 60-90 days out]
- New rate: $[NEW RATE]
- Justification (without over-explaining): scope changes, market,
  or just "time for a review after [X years]"
- What this price covers (reconfirm value)
- Out: they can accept or we can discuss an adjusted scope, or we
  can wind down the engagement cleanly

Tone: matter-of-fact, professional. This is a business decision, not
a favor I'm asking. Don't apologize for raising prices on services
I'm delivering well.

Common response patterns to be ready for:
- They accept (great)
- They try to negotiate (be willing to stay firm OR offer a
  smaller scope)
- They threaten to leave (let them — wrong-rate clients cost more
  than they're worth)
```

## BK-43: Quote a cleanup project

```
A prospect's QBO is [DESCRIBE THE MESS — e.g., "not reconciled in
18 months", "has 400 uncategorized transactions", "AR shows invoices
that were paid months ago"]. They want to hire me for cleanup before
starting ongoing monthly work.

Help me:
1. Estimate hours (walk through categorization vs. reconciliation vs.
   AR/AP cleanup vs. historical fixes)
2. Recommend a flat fee (not hourly — hourly rewards slowness)
3. Structure the engagement so I'm not stuck if it takes longer than
   expected
4. Draft the proposal email

Flat fee ranges in my market for this type of work:
- Minor cleanup (a few months out of date): $500-$1,000
- Moderate cleanup (6-12 months + some categorization issues):
  $1,000-$2,500
- Heavy cleanup (1+ year, messy chart of accounts, uncategorized
  mess): $2,500-$5,000

Pick the right tier and explain my reasoning.
```

## BK-44: Decline a client who can't afford me

```
A prospect explicitly said they can't afford my rates. They're a
[small business type] and genuinely would benefit from bookkeeping
help — I'm just not the right price point for them.

Write me a response that:
- Declines gracefully without making them feel bad
- Offers two lower-cost alternatives they might try:
  1. A shared bookkeeper platform like Bench, Pilot, or an offshore
     service
  2. Self-doing it with QBO + a CPA once a year to review
- Leaves me on good terms for when their business grows
- Doesn't over-qualify — no "maybe in the future we can work out
  something" if I don't actually mean it

Tone: kind, honest, respectful of their situation.
```
