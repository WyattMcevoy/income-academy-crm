# Prompts — Client Acquisition

Landing your first 3-5 clients. CPA referrals are the highest-ROI channel — most prompts target that.

## BK-01: Draft a CPA referral outreach email

```
Write a cold outreach email to a CPA firm asking to be their go-to
bookkeeping referral. My background:
- Based in [CITY, STATE]
- Specialize in [INDUSTRY NICHE — e.g., "small construction
  contractors" or "solopreneur consultants"]
- Current capacity: [X] clients (be honest — "I'm building to 3-5
  clients" is fine)
- Software: QBO ProAdvisor certified [or working toward it]
- Years of relevant experience: [YEARS]

The CPA firm I'm emailing:
- Name: [FIRM NAME]
- Size: [# partners if known, or "small practice" / "medium firm"]
- How I found them: [referral from [NAME] / Google search / LinkedIn]

Write the email:
- Subject line that doesn't look like spam (specific, not "partnership
  opportunity!")
- Opening line that acknowledges their time is valuable
- My pitch: I help their clients with the bookkeeping they don't want
  to do, which lets the CPA focus on higher-value tax + advisory work
- What's in it for them: reliable monthly books when clients come in
  for tax season (reduces their prep work)
- Soft ask: can I schedule a 15-min intro call to learn about what
  their referral process looks like?
- Professional sign-off

Tone: colleague-to-colleague, not sales-to-CPA. CPAs get pitched a
lot — stand out by being specific about what I'd do for them and
their clients, not vague about being "a great partner."
```

## BK-02: Follow-up after a CPA referral call

```
I had a 15-min intro call with [CPA NAME] at [FIRM] on [DATE]. They
were [WARM / LUKEWARM / NONCOMMITTAL] and said [WHAT THEY COMMITTED
TO, e.g., "they'd think about sending me one of their new clients
next month"].

Write a professional follow-up email:
- Thank them for the time (genuine, not performative)
- Reference ONE specific thing from the call (proves I listened)
- Confirm the next step they mentioned
- Offer a small value-add (a resource I can share, a referral I
  could offer them in return)
- Don't pester — leave them feeling good about the interaction,
  not pressured

If they were lukewarm, include a "no pressure" line that makes it
easy for them to not engage without feeling awkward about me later.
```

## BK-03: Introduction to a local networking group

```
Write a 2-minute introduction I can deliver at a [BNI meeting /
Chamber of Commerce breakfast / Rotary / industry meetup] to
introduce my bookkeeping service.

My pitch covers:
- Who I am (1 sentence)
- The specific problem I solve (small business owners who hate
  bookkeeping but need accurate books for taxes and cash flow)
- Who's a great referral for me: [SPECIFIC CLIENT PROFILE — e.g.,
  "small contractors with 5-15 employees in [AREA]"]
- How to refer someone: not "send them my way" but a specific
  memorable ask
- Something personal / memorable about me so they remember me next
  week

Tone: warm, confident, not polished-to-death. This is a small room of
local business owners, not a TED talk.
```

## BK-04: The "free books review" offer

```
Help me write a compelling "free 15-minute QBO review" offer I can
share with prospects. This is my best lead-conversion tool.

The offer:
- 15 minutes on a Zoom call
- I look at their QuickBooks Online file
- I identify 3 specific issues or opportunities
- I tell them plainly what I'd fix and what they could fix themselves
- No sales pitch at the end — I earn a potential engagement by
  being useful first

Write:
- Email subject line that doesn't look like a gimmick
- 2-paragraph outreach email I can send to prospects
- What I'd say at the start of the Zoom (sets expectations)
- What I'd say at the end if they're a fit (how to suggest an
  ongoing engagement without being pushy)
- What I'd say if they're NOT a fit (how to leave them with value
  and goodwill)

Tone: confident in the value, not desperate. If I present this like
a steal, it'll feel like one.
```

## BK-05: Responding to a "can you just look at my books real quick?" request

```
Someone at a networking event / in my social circle just said "hey,
can you just look at my books real quick and tell me what's wrong?"
They want free advice without an engagement.

Write a diplomatic response that:
- Doesn't burn the relationship
- Doesn't give away hours of work for free
- Offers a clear path forward (either the paid review, or an
  explanation of why quick-advice on bookkeeping rarely works)
- Keeps me in their mind for when they DO need a bookkeeper

Tone: warm, professional, with a clear soft boundary. This scenario
is frequent — nailing the response saves hours per month.
```
