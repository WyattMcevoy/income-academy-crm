# Prompts — Onboarding & Engagement

Setting up a new client cleanly. Done well, this prevents 90% of future scope disputes.

## BK-10: Draft an engagement letter for a new client

```
Help me draft an engagement letter for a new bookkeeping client:

Client info:
- Business name: [BIZ NAME]
- Business type: [LLC / S-Corp / sole prop]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY]
- Annual revenue estimate: [$X]
- Number of employees: [X]
- Current state of their books: [CLEAN / MESSY / NO BOOKS / QBO
  SET UP BUT NOT USED]

Scope I'm providing:
- Monthly transaction categorization and reconciliation
- Monthly financial reports (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow)
- Monthly summary narrative (a 1-paragraph plain-English summary)
- [ANY OTHER SERVICES — AR tracking? AP? payroll coordination? QBO
  cleanup? specify]

Scope I'm NOT providing (be clear on these):
- Tax preparation (they need their CPA for that)
- Tax advice
- Audit defense
- Payroll filing (if they need this, refer them to Gusto / a payroll
  company)
- Anything requiring a CPA or EA license

Fees: $[AMOUNT]/month, billed on [DATE]
Cancellation: either party with 30 days written notice

Write a complete engagement letter including:
- Scope of services (clear, specific, limited)
- What I need from them each month (access, receipts, questions
  answered within 3 business days)
- What they can expect from me (delivery timeline, communication
  cadence, availability)
- Pricing and payment terms
- Termination clause
- Limitation of liability
- Confidentiality
- Signature blocks

Tone: clear and professional. Not "Scary legal document" — more like
"Here's how we'll work together in writing."

Important: recommend the client have their attorney review before
signing, especially in states with specific rules. This is a
template for a bookkeeping engagement — not legal advice.
```

## BK-11: New client intake questionnaire

```
Help me build an intake questionnaire I send a new client BEFORE our
first meeting. Goals:
- Save time in the kickoff meeting
- Uncover any surprises (messy books, missing access, unrealistic
  expectations) before I commit
- Show the client I'm organized and serious

Create a questionnaire covering:
- Business basics (entity type, fiscal year, CPA name + contact)
- Current state of books (software, how current, last reconciled)
- Accounts I'll need access to (bank accounts, credit cards, payroll
  system, POS system, e-commerce platforms)
- Transaction volume estimate (transactions/month)
- Categories / chart of accounts (do they have a working COA or does
  it need cleanup)
- Deliverables they care about (P&L only? Detailed reports? Specific
  KPIs?)
- Communication preferences (email? Slack? scheduled calls?)
- Key dates (when do they need month-end reports by?)
- One open-ended question: "What's the single biggest bookkeeping
  headache you want solved?"

Format: numbered list, estimated 10 minutes to complete. Delivered
as a Google Form OR a fillable PDF OR a shared Google Doc (my choice).

Keep it friendly, not interrogation-style.
```

## BK-12: The kickoff meeting agenda

```
My new client signed the engagement letter. The kickoff meeting is
in 3 days. Write me a 45-minute kickoff agenda that:

1. [10 min] Review the engagement — reconfirm scope + expectations
   verbally (some clients haven't actually read the letter)
2. [15 min] Review their intake questionnaire answers — dig into
   anything unclear
3. [10 min] Technical access — walk through getting me into QBO /
   bank accounts / whatever systems we need. Use a password manager
   tool (not email).
4. [5 min] Set the monthly rhythm — when I'll start, when the first
   deliverable is due, what they'll see
5. [5 min] Q&A and what to expect in their first monthly report

Give me what to literally say at the start, how to pace each section,
and what to leave them with at the end.

Goal: they leave the meeting confident, impressed, and ready to
share data. I leave with everything I need to do good work.
```

## BK-13: Handle access requests diplomatically

```
I need to request admin access to my new client's QBO account to
do my work. Some clients are nervous about this ("you'll see
everything!"). Write me:

1. An email explaining WHY I need admin access (not view-only)
2. What I will and won't do with it (no changes without approval,
   no access beyond QBO)
3. How to grant it (step-by-step QBO instructions they can follow)
4. A reassurance paragraph about data security + confidentiality

Include: my standing offer to screen-share the first access grant
so they watch me set myself up, nothing happens off-screen.

Tone: patient, not defensive. They're right to be cautious.
```
