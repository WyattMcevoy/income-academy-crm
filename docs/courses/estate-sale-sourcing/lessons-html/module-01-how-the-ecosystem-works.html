The 4 types of "sales" you'll source from

Not all sales are equal. Here are the 4 types and which to focus on.

1. Estate sales (the gold mine)

A professional estate sale company is hired by a family (or estate executor) to sell off the contents of a home. Usually after:

  • A death
  • A move to assisted living
  • A divorce
  • A "downsizing" decision

Why they're great:

  • Whole-house worth of stuff
  • Professional pricing (usually negotiable)
  • Items priced to sell over 1 to 2 days

2. Garage sales (the random treasure)

Run by the homeowners themselves. Cash only. No professional pricing.

Why they're great:

  • Cheap prices (homeowners want stuff GONE)
  • Surprises - you never know what you'll find
  • Often willing to negotiate aggressively

Why they're tricky:

  • Quality varies wildly
  • Smaller inventory
  • More driving for fewer items

3. Auction houses (advanced - skip for now)

Live or online auctions of specific items.

Higher-stakes. Faster pace. We don't recommend this for your first 6 months.

4. Thrift stores (decent backup)

Goodwill, Salvation Army, local thrift stores. Item-by-item pricing, usually higher than estate sales.

Why we DON'T focus on these:

  • Already-picked over by other resellers
  • Higher prices than estate sales
  • Hit-or-miss quality

Fine as a backup. Don't make it your main strategy.

Who runs estate sales

Estate sale companies. There are usually 5 to 15 of them in any major metro area, plus dozens of smaller operators.

The company:

  1. Gets hired by a family
  2. Catalogs the contents of the home (1 to 2 weeks of work)
  3. Prices everything (their judgment - some are better than others)
  4. Advertises the sale (usually 1 to 2 weeks before)
  5. Runs the sale Friday-Saturday-Sunday (or some subset)
  6. Takes 25-40% of the gross as their fee

This is important to understand because:

  • Some companies price competently. Others wildly under-price or over-price.
  • The under-pricers are your favorites. You'll learn which companies they are within 4 to 6 weeks.
  • Friday is "first day, full price." Saturday is "20% off most items." Sunday is "everything 50% off."

Most professional resellers go on Saturdays - the sweet spot of inventory still available + meaningful discounts.

The 5 places to find sales every weekend

Bookmark these. Check them every Thursday and Friday.

1. EstateSales.net (THE big one)

Go to: estatesales.net

Type your zip code. You'll see all upcoming sales within whatever radius you set.

Each sale listing shows:

  • Address
  • Dates and times
  • 30 to 100 photos of items
  • Sale company

Spend 15 minutes Friday afternoon scrolling photos. Decide which sales look promising for your niche.

2. EstateSales.org (a competitor)

Different listings (mostly). Always check both.

3. AuctionZip.com (if you also want auctions later)

Mostly auctions, but lists some estate sales too.

4. Local Facebook groups

Search Facebook for "[your town] estate sales" or "[your town] garage sales." Join 2 to 4 groups.

These are great for:

  • Garage sales (which don't show up on the .net sites)
  • Last-minute sales added by smaller companies

5. Craigslist

Yes, Craigslist still works. Go to your city's Craigslist, click "garage sales" under "for sale."

Scan Friday and Saturday.

The Friday afternoon planning routine

This is the routine that turns "wandering Saturday mornings" into "profitable Saturday mornings."

Step 1: Check estatesales.net (15 min)

Type your zip. Set radius to 30 minutes drive max for now. Look at this weekend's sales.

For each sale, ask:

  • Does it look like a "good house"? (lots of stuff in nice condition vs. picked-over)
  • Are there photos of categories you target? (more on categories in Module 3)
  • Is the address reasonable for your route?

Mark 3 to 5 sales as "yes, going."

Step 2: Check Facebook groups (10 min)

Look for garage sales added by neighbors. Note any that look interesting.

Step 3: Map the route (10 min)

Open Google Maps. Add your 4 to 5 sales as stops. Order them by start time (sales open at staggered times - some at 8am, some at 9am, some at 10am).

Save the route to your phone.

Step 4: Pack the bag (5 min)

Bring:

  • Cash (small bills - $300 to $500 for your first run)
  • Tape measure
  • Phone with the route saved
  • Reusable shopping bag

That's it. You're ready for Saturday.

What to expect at your first sale

The first time you walk into an estate sale, it's overwhelming. That's normal. Here's what to know.

Arrive 15 to 30 minutes before opening

There will be a line. Some companies hand out numbers to organize entry. Other sales are first-come-first-served chaos.

Walk in with a plan

Don't just gawk. Walk through the WHOLE house once before buying anything. This takes 15 to 20 minutes.

You're scanning for:

  • Categories you target (we cover these in Module 3)
  • Things that look high-end or unusual
  • Items that look mispriced (to your benefit)

Take photos

You're allowed to (almost everyone does). Take photos of anything you might want to research before buying. Then walk away to a quiet spot and Google it.

This is how you avoid overpaying.

Negotiate respectfully

On Friday: small negotiation only. Most companies won't budge.

On Saturday: 10 to 20% off is normal to ask for, especially on items over $50. Be polite.

On Sunday: 30 to 50% off is normal on the half-price day.

Don't be greedy. A good relationship with the sale company means they'll save items for you next time.

Pay and load

Most companies accept cash, check, or sometimes Venmo/Zelle. Cash is always welcomed.

Load your car carefully. Bring blankets to wrap fragile items.

If you got stuck

"There aren't any estate sales in my area"

Two possibilities:

  1. You're in a rural area. Try expanding your search radius to 60 or 90 minutes. Most rural sourcers travel further.
  2. Estate sales are seasonal in some places. Check garage sales (more year-round) until estate sale season picks up.

"The sales near me look picked-over already"

Two possibilities:

  1. You're going on Sundays (last day) - try Friday or Saturday morning instead.
  2. There's a lot of competition in your area - good news: that means there's enough money to compete for. You just need a better eye (Module 3) to find what others miss.

"I went to a sale and felt overwhelmed"

Normal. Your first 3 sales feel chaotic. By sale #5 you'll start seeing patterns. By sale #10 you'll feel comfortable.

Don't quit after 1 or 2. Give yourself 4 to 6 weekends before judging.
