Module 3: What to Look For - The 7 Categories

Time: about 60 minutes What you'll do: Learn the 7 categories of items that consistently profit, and what specifically to look for in each.

Welcome back

This is the most important module in the course.

Most beginners walk into estate sales and look at EVERYTHING. They get overwhelmed, buy random things, and end up with a garage full of unsellable inventory.

Pros walk into estate sales and look at SPECIFIC THINGS. They walk through quickly, find the items in their categories, and leave.

By the end of this module, you'll be a pro.

The 7 categories that consistently profit

Most estate sale resellers pick 2 to 3 of these and specialize. Don't try to do all 7 at once.

  1. Vintage kitchen items (Pyrex, CorningWare, vintage tools)
  2. Vintage tools (woodworking, mechanical, hardware)
  3. Vintage clothing and textiles
  4. Mid-century furniture and decor
  5. Vintage toys and games
  6. Records, books, and ephemera
  7. Vintage jewelry and watches

We'll cover each in depth. As you read, note which 2 to 3 feel most natural to you.

Category 1: Vintage Kitchen Items

Why it works

  • High volume at most estate sales (every house has a kitchen)
  • Easy to identify (you grew up with it)
  • Reliable demand (collectors and decorators always buying)
  • Fast-moving on eBay

What specifically sells

Pyrex (1950s-1970s) Look for the colored or patterned Pyrex bowls and casserole dishes. The Pyrex with patterns like "Butterprint," "Snowflake," "Gooseberry" - some pieces sell for $100 to $500+.

CorningWare (1960s-1980s) Casserole dishes with the blue cornflower pattern. The bigger pieces (3-quart and up) sell for $30 to $150.

Vintage Pyrex measuring cups, mixing bowls, fridgies Even simple clear Pyrex with red graphics sells for $20 to $80 each.

Vintage Tupperware Especially original 1960s-1970s Tupperware. Avocado green and harvest gold sell well to nostalgic buyers.

Cast iron pans Pre-1960 cast iron from Griswold, Wagner, or Lodge. Worth $40 to $400+ depending on size and condition.

Le Creuset enameled cast iron Vintage Le Creuset (especially flame orange) sells for $80 to $300.

What to skip

  • Modern kitchen gadgets (Cuisinart, Ninja, etc.)
  • Plastic items from 1990s-2010s
  • Generic glass items with no maker mark

Pricing rule of thumb

If you can buy a piece for less than 25% of its eBay sold price, buy it.

Category 2: Vintage Tools

Why it works

  • Older men accumulate tools over decades
  • Modern collectors love quality vintage hand tools
  • Often under-priced at estate sales (sale companies don't know what to charge)
  • Strong eBay market

What specifically sells

Vintage hand planes (Stanley, Bailey, Bedrock) A clean Stanley #4 plane: $40 to $80. A Bedrock #604: $200 to $400.

Vintage chisels and saws Marples chisels, Disston saws (handsaws), pre-1950 specialty saws. $20 to $150 per item.

Vintage measuring tools (Starrett, Mitutoyo) Brass squares, machinists' squares, vintage levels. $30 to $200.

Mechanic's tools (Snap-On, Mac, vintage Craftsman) Older Snap-On and Mac wrenches. Pre-1980 Craftsman tools (made in USA). $5 to $80 per piece.

Garden tools (Oxo, vintage trowels, pruning shears) Quality wooden-handled trowels. Vintage pruning shears. $10 to $60.

What to skip

  • Modern Harbor Freight or generic tools
  • Rusted-beyond-repair items (unless extremely rare)
  • Power tools (heavier to ship, buyer pickup hard to find)

Pricing rule of thumb

Buy any quality vintage hand tool for $5 to $20. Pretty much always profitable.

Category 3: Vintage Clothing and Textiles

Why it works

  • Vintage clothing has skyrocketed in value
  • Y2K nostalgia driving 1990s-2000s prices up
  • Unique pieces with no comparable buy elsewhere
  • Lightweight, easy to ship

What specifically sells

Vintage band t-shirts Pre-2000 concert t-shirts. Some sell for $200 to $2,000+ depending on band and rarity.

Vintage sportswear 1980s-1990s Nike, Adidas, Champion sweatshirts. $40 to $300.

Vintage denim Pre-1990s Levi's (especially 501s, 505s with red tab). $80 to $400.

Designer pieces Vintage Coach bags, Pendleton wool, Patagonia from 1980s-1990s. $50 to $500.

Vintage textiles Hand-quilted blankets, vintage embroidered linens, decorative tablecloths. $30 to $200.

What to skip

  • Polyester from 1970s (low resale value)
  • Stained or heavily damaged items
  • Generic 2000s-2010s clothing (already saturated on resale)

Pricing rule of thumb

Vintage clothing is highly variable. Always check eBay sold listings before buying anything over $20.

Category 4: Mid-Century Furniture and Decor

Why it works

  • Mid-century modern (1950s-1970s) is in high demand
  • Specific brands (Knoll, Herman Miller, Eames) command premium prices
  • Decor items easy to ship (lamps, art, smaller pieces)

What specifically sells

Eames-era chairs Even non-original mid-century chairs in good condition sell for $100 to $400.

Vintage lamps Mid-century brass or ceramic table lamps: $40 to $200.

Vintage art and prints Original signed prints, mid-century lithographs. $30 to $300.

Decorative items Brass candlesticks, vintage glassware, ceramic vases. $15 to $80 each.

Vintage textiles Mid-century rugs, tapestries, decorative throws. $30 to $250.

What to skip

  • Heavy furniture (shipping is impossible - local pickup only, harder to sell)
  • Generic 1980s furniture (low resale market)
  • Anything that needs reupholstering (cost outweighs profit)

Pricing rule of thumb

For furniture: only buy what you can sell locally (Facebook Marketplace, OfferUp). Don't buy heavy items thinking you'll ship them.

Category 5: Vintage Toys and Games

Why it works

  • Boomers and Gen X collectors paying premium for childhood items
  • Strong nostalgia market
  • Lightweight, easy to ship

What specifically sells

Vintage Star Wars figures (1977-1985) Even loose figures: $5 to $50. Rare ones: $100 to $1,000+.

Vintage board games Pre-1990 Monopoly editions, vintage Scrabble, Risk, etc. $20 to $100.

Vintage Barbie dolls (1959-1980) Loose, played-with: $20 to $80. Mint condition: $200 to $2,000+.

Hot Wheels, Matchbox cars (pre-1980) $3 to $30 each, sometimes more for rare ones.

Vintage video games and consoles Atari 2600 with games: $50 to $300. NES games: $5 to $200 each.

What to skip

  • Modern toys (already saturated on eBay)
  • Damaged or incomplete games
  • Toys with missing parts

Pricing rule of thumb

Always check eBay sold listings before buying any individual toy over $10.

Category 6: Records, Books, and Ephemera

Why it works

  • Vinyl records have made a comeback
  • Specific books (cookbooks, art books, first editions) hold value
  • Ephemera (vintage advertisements, postcards, photos) has niche but loyal collectors

What specifically sells

Vinyl records Specific genres: jazz, classic rock, hip-hop, soul. $5 to $200 per record depending on rarity.

Vintage cookbooks 1950s-1970s community cookbooks, signed first editions. $10 to $100.

Vintage art books Out-of-print photography books, art reference books. $20 to $200.

Comic books (pre-1990) Avoid unless you're educated - prices are wildly variable. Easy to overpay.

Vintage postcards, photographs Specific eras and themes (military, train, transportation). $2 to $30 each.

What to skip

  • Modern paperbacks
  • Damaged or moldy books
  • Generic vinyl records (most are worth $1)

Pricing rule of thumb

Records: bring the Discogs app to look up prices in real-time at sales. Books: only buy if you recognize the title or author. Generic books rarely profit.

Category 7: Vintage Jewelry and Watches

Why it works

  • Often hidden gems at estate sales
  • Even costume jewelry has resale value
  • Watches especially can be huge profit (don't skip just because you don't know watches)

What specifically sells

Sterling silver jewelry Look for "925" or "Sterling" stamps. Even simple sterling pieces: $20 to $100.

Vintage costume jewelry (signed) Eisenberg, Trifari, Coro pieces. $20 to $150.

Vintage watches Brand-name watches: Hamilton, Bulova, Timex. $30 to $300. Mechanical watches that work: $50 to $1,000+. WARNING: NEVER pay more than $50 for a watch unless you know what you're doing. Easy to overpay.

Vintage bead and pearl strands Real pearls and quality beads. $30 to $200.

What to skip

  • Items without clear maker marks (hard to value)
  • Modern fashion jewelry
  • Items that need extensive restoration

Pricing rule of thumb

Sterling silver: weigh it. Spot price for silver gives you a baseline (this changes daily, but search "silver spot price"). Watches: until you've studied watches, always assume the worst-case value.

How to pick YOUR 2-3 categories

Stop reading. Get a piece of paper.

Ask yourself:

1. Which categories sound most fun to research?

You'll spend hours on eBay looking up prices. Pick categories you'd enjoy researching.

2. Which match your existing knowledge?

Cooked for decades? Vintage kitchen items. Worked on cars? Vintage tools. Loved music? Records. Mom of teens with vintage tastes? Clothing.

3. Which match your storage situation?

Furniture takes garage space. Clothing fits in a closet. Tools fit in a small corner.

Pick categories that fit your storage.

4. Which are easiest to sell where you live?

Heavy furniture: only sells locally (need an active local market). Clothing/jewelry/small items: sells easily on eBay nationally.

If you don't have a strong local market, lean toward shippable categories.

If you got stuck

"I can't pick - I'm interested in like 5 categories"

Default to vintage kitchen + vintage tools (broadly available, easy to identify, fast to research). Add a third later if you want.

"I don't know how to research prices on eBay"

Walk-through:

  1. Go to ebay.com
  2. Search for the item (e.g., "vintage Pyrex Butterprint bowl")
  3. Click "Filter" -> "Sold items"
  4. Look at SOLD prices, not asking prices. Sold = real market price.
  5. Note the price range. This is what you can expect to sell for.

This 30-second check before buying is the most important habit in this whole course.
