# Vintage Tools — Quick Reference Cheatsheet

Top makers and what to look for at estate sales. Keep on phone while sourcing.

---

## Stanley

America's dominant tool maker 1857-present. Pre-1970 USA-made Stanleys are the collectibles; post-1970 is commodity.

**Highest-value items:**
- **No. 4 / 4½ bench planes** (Type 1-15, roughly 1869-1935): $75-300
- **No. 45 combination planes** (1884-1962): $150-500 complete with cutters
- **No. 55 universal plane** (1897-1962): $300-800 — the "king of combination planes"
- **No. 1 and No. 2 small planes** (rare): $200-1,000+
- **Bedrock series** (prefix "60½", "604", etc.): all collectible, $100-400

**What to look for:**
- Cast "STANLEY" logo on the bed (no sticker = older = better)
- Frog (adjustable piece holding the blade) matches era of plane
- Original wood handles, no replacements
- "Made in USA" stamp in the casting
- Patent dates cast into the bed indicate era

**Red flags:**
- Modern plastic replacements
- "Made in England" post-1970
- Crudely stamped "Stanley" with block lettering (usually reproductions)

---

## Starrett

Precision tools — Athol, Massachusetts, 1880-present. Machinists prize them.

**Highest-value items:**
- Combination squares (#11H, #12, #4R): $40-200 each depending on set completeness
- Surface gauges: $75-200
- Vintage calipers and micrometers (pre-1980): $50-150
- Complete machinist's chests with Starrett contents: $200-1,500

**What to look for:**
- "STARRETT" in cursive script (stamped or engraved, not printed)
- Made in USA stamp
- Proper fit and feel — worn but smooth is fine, sticky is bad

---

## Lie-Nielsen Toolworks

Modern premium maker (1981-present, Maine). Used Lie-Nielsens hold value because new prices are high.

**What to look for:**
- Bronze planes typically $150-400 used
- Any Lie-Nielsen in good condition will sell — the brand is strong
- Check the bronze patina (don't polish it — collectors prefer aged)

---

## Millers Falls

Cheaper than Stanley, often overlooked, which makes it great at estate sales.

**Highest-value items:**
- Eggbeater hand drills (models 2, 2A, 97): $30-120
- Bench planes (similar to Stanley Bedrock series): $60-200
- Brass-bound levels: $40-150

---

## Other makers worth knowing

- **Wood River** — modern Chinese-made, mostly user grade ($30-80)
- **Record** — English maker, similar to Stanley. Marked "Record" on bed. ($40-200)
- **Bailey** — actually an early Stanley line (merger in 1869). Pre-1869 Baileys are rare.
- **Sargent** — USA mid-tier, undervalued. ($30-150)
- **Disston** (saws) — pre-1940 Disston handsaws: $40-200
- **Starrett / L.S. Starrett** — precision
- **Brown & Sharpe** — precision, older and rarer than Starrett

---

## Red flags that something is a reproduction

- "China" stamp or sticker
- Crudely cast logo with rough edges
- Missing expected patent dates
- Plastic parts where metal should be
- Wrong weight (real vintage iron is heavier than you'd expect)
- Too-perfect condition for the claimed era

---

## How to quickly check a plane's era

Flip the plane over. Look for:
1. Patent dates cast into the bed behind the frog
2. Logo style (curved cursive = older, block = newer)
3. Lateral lever (shape changed over decades)
4. Frog seating (Bedrock has a different frog design from regular Bailey)

Spend 15 min with [Patrick Leach's Stanley Type Studies page](http://supertool.com/stanleyblog/) for era identification. Most experienced buyers use this as their reference.

---

## At a sale: the 30-second check

1. Pick up the plane / tool
2. Does it say "USA" or "Made in England" (= newer, less valuable)? → lower bid
3. Is there a cursive Stanley / Starrett logo? → likely pre-1970, potentially valuable
4. Are original parts present? No plastic, no replacements?
5. Asking price vs. your 40% rule based on eBay sold range? Buy or walk.

Good finds are usually priced $5-25 at estate sales. Once prices go over $50, the seller likely knows what they have.
