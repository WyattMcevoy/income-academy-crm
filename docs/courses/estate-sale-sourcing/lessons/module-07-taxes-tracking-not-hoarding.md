## Welcome back

Three problems that derail estate sale resellers:

1. **No tracking.** They have no idea if they're actually profitable.
2. **Tax surprises.** They get a 1099 from eBay and panic.
3. **Hoarding.** They keep buying inventory that doesn't sell, garage gets full.

This module fixes all three.

---

## Tracking your numbers

You need a simple system to track:

- What you bought (cost + date)
- Where you bought it
- What you listed it for
- What it sold for
- Total profit per item

### Option 1: Simple Google Sheet (recommended)

Columns:

```
Date | Item | Source | Cost | Listed Price | Sold Price | Fees | Profit | Notes
```

For each item, fill in the row over time. **5 minutes per week to update.**

### Option 2: Use a tool like GoDaddy Bookkeeping or QuickBooks Self-Employed

About $10/month. Auto-syncs eBay sales. Tracks expenses.

Useful at higher volume (100+ items/month). Overkill for beginners.

### Option 3: eBay's built-in seller hub

eBay tracks your sales for you. **But not your COSTS.**

So you still need a separate record of what each item cost you.

### What to track for each sale

When something sells:

- Final sold price (eBay shows this)
- eBay/PayPal fees (about 13-15% total)
- Shipping cost (you paid)
- Your original cost (from your sourcing notes)

Profit = Sold price - eBay fees - Shipping cost - Your cost

Track this for EVERY sale. It's the only way to know if your model is working.

## Taxes (the part nobody wants to talk about)

If you sell more than $600 in a year on eBay, **you'll get a 1099 form.** This goes to the IRS.

You don't have a choice about reporting it. **Plan now to avoid surprises.**

### What you owe

You owe income tax on PROFIT, not on revenue.

Profit = Total sales - All costs (including sourcing, shipping, supplies, fees)

If you bought a Pyrex bowl for $5, sold it for $50, paid $7 fees, paid $8 shipping... you have $30 in profit.

### Set up a separate "tax" savings account

Every month:

- Calculate your profit for the month
- Set aside 25-30% in a separate savings account
- This is your "tax money" - don't touch it

When tax time comes, you'll have the cash ready.

### Estimated quarterly taxes

Once you're making consistent income, you may need to pay quarterly estimated taxes.

The IRS has 4 deadlines per year (April, June, September, January). If you owe more than $1,000 in tax, you're supposed to pay quarterly.

**Talk to a CPA when you cross $5,000 in annual profit.** A 1-hour consultation costs $200 and saves you from mistakes.

### Deductions you can claim

Track these costs - they're deductions:

- Sourcing inventory (cost of items you bought to resell)
- eBay/PayPal fees
- Shipping costs (boxes, tape, postage)
- Mileage to estate sales (about $0.65/mile for 2026)
- Storage equipment (shelves, bins)
- Camera, lighting, photography supplies
- Home office (a portion of your house bills - if you use a dedicated space)

**Save receipts.** A photo on your phone counts.

## Avoiding the hoarder trap

The dirty secret of estate sale sourcing: **most people end up with garages full of stuff that doesn't sell.**

This is preventable.

### Rule 1: First-in-first-out (FIFO)

Items sit in your garage too long. They lose value (you forget about them, they get damaged).

When you bring home new inventory, list it within 2 weeks. Period.

### Rule 2: The 60-day rule

If something hasn't sold in 60 days:

- Drop the price 20%
- After another 30 days, drop another 20%
- After 90 days total, decide: keep, donate, or take to a flea market

**Don't keep things for "someday."** Move them.

### Rule 3: Track inventory days

In your Google Sheet, add a column for "days since listed."

Sort by this column once per month. Anything over 60 days needs attention.

### Rule 4: Know your storage capacity

A 4-foot section of garage shelf holds about 30 items. A whole garage section: 100 to 200.

When you fill your space, **stop sourcing until you sell some.** Don't expand storage just to fit more inventory.

### Rule 5: The "would I buy this again?" test

Once a quarter, walk through your inventory. For each item, ask:

> "If I saw this at an estate sale tomorrow, would I buy it for what I paid?"

If no -> it was a mistake. Donate or fire-sale.

## When to take inventory at a flea market

Flea markets are how you turn slow inventory into cash.

### When it's worth doing

- You have 50+ items that haven't moved on eBay
- A flea market is within an hour drive
- You can spare a Saturday

### How it works

1. Pay $30 to $80 for a vendor space
2. Set up at 7am
3. Sell items at 50% to 75% of your eBay listed price
4. Cash only, no shipping
5. Pack up at 2pm

You'll typically clear $300 to $800 per flea market day. **Lower margin per item, but cash now.**

### When NOT to do it

- You're worried it'll feel embarrassing (it's not - your customers will be friendly)
- You haven't sorted your inventory (don't bring junk - bring 80% of your decent stuff)
- It's your busy season for eBay (December-January don't bother - sell on eBay instead)

## If you got stuck

### "I'm scared of taxes"

You're allowed to feel that. Most people feel that way.

But: **the IRS is not coming for an estate sale reseller.** They want their share, and they're reasonable as long as you report honestly.

**Save 25-30% of profits in a separate account.** Talk to a CPA when you cross $5K in annual profit. Done.

### "I have way too much inventory and feel overwhelmed"

The first step: **list 5 of your slow movers TODAY.** Get them moving even at a loss.

The second step: stop sourcing for 30 days while you clean up.

The third step: re-evaluate which categories are working and which aren't (Module 3 review).
