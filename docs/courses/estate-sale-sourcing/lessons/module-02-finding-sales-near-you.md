## The Friday afternoon routine (30 to 45 minutes)

Block this time on your calendar. **Same time every Friday.**

### 4:00 PM: Check estatesales.net

Open your browser. Go to `estatesales.net`. Type your zip.

Filter by:

- Distance: 30 to 45 minutes drive max (start small, expand later)
- Dates: this weekend only

Scroll through every sale. **Read photos like a book.** You're looking for:

- Lots of stuff (vs. a half-empty house)
- Variety (kitchen, garage, basement all visible)
- Categories you target (Module 3 covers this)
- Reasonable prices in the photos (some companies will pre-price items in photos)

For each promising sale, click "Save" or note the URL.

### 4:30 PM: Check Facebook groups

Open Facebook. Go to the local groups you joined in Module 1.

Scroll for:
- Garage sales added in the last 24 hours
- Last-minute estate sales not on the .net sites

Note any worth visiting.

### 4:45 PM: Map the route

Open Google Maps. Add each sale as a stop.

**Order them by start time, not distance.** A sale starting at 8am is non-negotiable - you need to be first in line. A sale starting at 10am can wait.

Add 30 minutes between sales (drive + browse time).

### Example Saturday route

```
7:30 AM - Leave home
8:00 AM - Sale #1 (estate, opens 8am - get there first)
9:00 AM - Sale #2 (estate, opens 9am)
10:00 AM - Sale #3 (garage, started Friday so already open)
11:30 AM - Sale #4 (estate, opens 11am)
12:30 PM - Done. Drive home.
1:00 PM - Lunch and unpack
```

5 hours total. 4 to 5 sales. **That's a productive Saturday.**

## How many sales should you do per Saturday?

Beginners: 3 to 4 sales.

Intermediates (2 to 3 months in): 5 to 7 sales.

Pros: 8 to 12 (they're FAST at scanning).

**Quality over quantity for your first 30 days.** Going to 3 sales and reviewing them carefully beats rushing through 8.

## How to read an estate sale listing

This is the skill that separates beginners from pros: **knowing which sales are worth your time before you drive there.**

### Look for these green flags

**1. Lots of photos (50+)**

A company that takes 50+ photos has done their homework. Their pricing tends to be more accurate.

**2. Variety of categories visible**

Kitchen items, garage tools, basement antiques, attic vintage. The more variety, the more you'll find your specific category.

**3. "Decades of collecting"**

The listing says "estate of a lifelong collector" or "decades of antiques and collectibles." This is the gold mine.

**4. Visible quality items in photos**

Pyrex, vintage tools, antique furniture, signed items. If you can SEE good stuff in the photos, there's more inside.

### Look for these red flags

**1. Few photos (under 20)**

Either a small house or a lazy company. Probably skip unless it's close to home.

**2. Everything looks "picked"**

If photos show empty shelves and bare furniture, the family already removed the good stuff before the sale company arrived. Skip.

**3. Crazy-high prices in photos**

Some companies price items at full retail. Walk away or come back Sunday for half-off.

**4. "1980s and newer"**

Most resellable items are pre-1980 (vintage). A house full of 1990s and 2000s stuff has less to flip.

## Building your "company favorites" list

Within 4 to 6 weekends, you'll start noticing patterns. Some sale companies consistently:

- Under-price items (good for you)
- Over-price items (avoid until Sunday half-off)
- Have great inventory (worth driving farther)
- Are tough negotiators (set expectations)

**Keep a simple Google Doc** titled "Estate Sale Companies" with notes:

```
Vintage Estate Sales LLC:
- Quality: 8/10
- Pricing: usually fair, willing to negotiate
- Last visited: 2026-04-12
- Notes: love their pricing on kitchen items, tough on antiques

ABC Estate Sales:
- Quality: 5/10
- Pricing: aggressive (high)
- Last visited: 2026-04-19
- Notes: only worth visiting on Sunday half-off day
```

After 3 months, you'll know which companies to drive 90 minutes for and which to skip entirely.

## When to skip a Saturday

Some Saturdays just aren't worth it. **Don't force it.**

Skip if:

- Only 1 to 2 sales available within driving range
- Weather is bad (sales get rained out, plus driving is unsafe)
- All sales are on the half-off Sunday side - just go Sunday
- You're sick or burned out

You don't have to source every weekend. **Better to do 3 great Saturdays per month than 4 mediocre ones.**

## If you got stuck

### "I planned a route but only found 2 sales worth visiting"

That's actually fine. **Better than forcing 4 mediocre stops.** Visit the 2 good ones thoroughly. You can stop at thrift stores or check Craigslist for last-minute garage sales on the way home.

### "I keep arriving at sales late"

Friday night, set your alarm 30 minutes earlier than you think. Saturday traffic is lighter than weekday traffic - you'll arrive on time even with the early alarm.

The pros are at the first sale 15 minutes early. **Be early.**

### "I don't have time on Friday afternoons"

Try Friday evening (8pm to 9pm). Or Saturday at 6am if you're a morning person.

The point isn't WHEN you plan - it's that you DO plan. Skipping the planning step is the #1 reason beginners burn out.

### "The sales in my area are saturated with other resellers"

Welcome to a healthy market. **More resellers = more money flowing through your local market.**

The way to compete: get there earlier (line up before the doors open), focus on categories OTHER resellers ignore, and build relationships with the sale companies.
