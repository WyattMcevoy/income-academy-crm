The 4 parties involved

There are exactly 4 players in any affiliate transaction.

1. The merchant

The company selling the product. Examples: Amazon, Wayfair, Bluehost, a small online cookie company.

2. The affiliate (you)

The person or website that recommends the product. That's you.

3. The customer

The actual buyer.

4. The network (sometimes)

A middle layer that connects affiliates and merchants. Examples: ShareASale, Impact, CJ Affiliate. Some merchants run their own programs without a network. Either is fine.

How they all connect

Merchant -- (offers commission via network or directly) -- You
                                                            |
                                                   (You publish content)
                                                            |
                                                       Customer
                                                            |
                                                  (Customer clicks your link)
                                                            |
                                                  (Customer buys from merchant)
                                                            |
                                                  (Merchant pays YOU commission)

That's the whole chain. One transaction, four parties.

How tracking actually works

This is the part most courses skip. Let's slow down here.

When you write a blog post and link to "this is the cookie sheet I love" - the link looks like this:

https://amazon.com/dp/B0EXAMPLE?tag=yourblog-20

The tag=yourblog-20 part is YOUR affiliate ID. When someone clicks that link, Amazon's servers note: "This person came from yourblog-20."

If they buy something within the next 24 hours (Amazon's window), you get a commission.

Cookies (in plain English)

When someone clicks your link, the merchant places a small file on their computer called a cookie. The cookie says: "This person came from yourblog-20 at this date and time."

If they come back later (within the cookie window) and buy something, the cookie tells the merchant who to credit.

Cookie windows by program

Program Cookie window
Amazon Associates 24 hours
Wayfair 7 days
Most major brands 30 days
Some software/SaaS 60 to 90 days

Longer cookie window = more chance you get paid for purchases that happen days after the click.

This is a big deal. A 24-hour Amazon window means you only earn if they buy that day. A 30-day Wayfair window means you can earn from a purchase made 4 weeks later. Higher-commission, longer-cookie programs are usually better than Amazon for the same effort.

Last-click attribution

Most programs use "last-click" attribution, meaning: the most recent affiliate link the customer clicked is the one that gets credit.

If they click YOUR link first, but then click someone else's link an hour later before buying - the OTHER affiliate gets paid.

This isn't fair, but it's the rule. It just means you should always provide value AND a reason to click your link, not theirs.

What "commission" actually means

Commissions are usually one of three structures:

Percentage commissions

You get a % of the sale. Examples:

  • Amazon: 1% to 10% depending on category (often 3% to 5%)
  • Wayfair: 7%
  • Most software products: 20% to 50%

Flat commissions

You get a fixed dollar amount per sale. Examples:

  • Bluehost (web hosting): $65 per sign-up
  • Some financial products: $50 to $200 per sign-up
  • Some SaaS tools: $5 to $50 per month for as long as the customer pays

Recurring commissions

This is the gold standard. You get paid every month the customer keeps paying. Common in:

  • Email marketing tools (ConvertKit, ActiveCampaign): 30% recurring
  • Web hosting (some): monthly recurring
  • Membership sites: 20% to 40% recurring

Recurring is best. One referral becomes income for years.

How you actually get paid

Most programs work like this:

Step 1: You earn commissions throughout the month

Your dashboard shows running totals. Most programs update daily.

Step 2: Commissions go through a "hold" period

This is the merchant's chance to confirm the customer didn't return the product. Typical hold: 30 days for physical products, 60 days for SaaS.

Step 3: After the hold, commissions are payable

The amount moves from "pending" to "approved."

Step 4: You hit the payment threshold

Most programs require a minimum balance before they pay. Amazon: $10. Most others: $50 to $100.

Step 5: Payment lands

Methods vary:

  • Direct deposit (most common)
  • PayPal
  • Check (some old-school programs still do this)

Typical timeline: 60 to 90 days from "click" to "money in your bank account."

That feels slow. It is slow. But once your engine is running, you've got money landing every month from work you did months ago.

What this looks like in practice

Let me walk you through one real example.

You write a blog post titled: "Best Cookie Sheets for Holiday Baking (Tested by a Mom of 5)."

The post:

  • Reviews 5 specific cookie sheets honestly
  • Explains why one is best for thin cookies, another for thick ones
  • Includes Amazon affiliate links to all 5

The post takes you 3 hours to write (with AI help) and goes live on November 1.

In December:

  • 200 people read the post
  • 30 click an Amazon link
  • 8 buy something within 24 hours (the cookie window)
  • Amazon pays you 4% commission on those 8 sales

Average sale: $25. Total sales: $200. Your commission: $8.

That's it. One $8 commission for 3 hours of work.

That sounds bad. Here's why it's actually great:

  • The post stays online forever
  • Year 2: 1,000 people read it. $40 commission.
  • Year 3: 3,000 people read it. $120 commission.
  • Year 4 (after holiday season picks up): 8,000 people read it. $320 commission.

Now multiply by 50 posts. Now you're looking at $5,000+ in commissions per year from posts you wrote once.

That's the affiliate marketing game. Slow start, compounding finish.

If you got stuck

"Cookie windows are confusing"

Simple version: when someone clicks your link, the merchant remembers them for X days. If they buy within X days, you get credit.

That's it.

"Why does Amazon's commission start so low?"

Amazon's volume is huge. Even at 4%, you make money if you have traffic. The trade-off is they're easy to start with (everyone trusts Amazon) but the per-sale commission is low.

We'll cover higher-commission programs in Module 5.

"What if a customer clicks my link and someone else's link?"

Whoever's link they clicked LAST gets the commission. That's "last-click attribution."

This is why your content should be the LAST thing they read before buying - not the first thing they read at the start of their research.
