What makes a good niche

A good niche has all 4 of these. Not 3. Not 5. Exactly 4.

1. Genuine interest from you

You should be able to read about this topic for fun. If you're forcing yourself to research it, you'll quit.

Test: Could you talk to a friend about this niche for 30 minutes without getting bored? If yes, pass.

2. Existing commercial activity

People buy products in this niche. Money flows through it.

Test: Search your niche on Amazon. Are there 50+ products? Are they priced at $20+? If yes, pass.

If your niche is "free advice on how to be a good neighbor" - no products, no money.

3. Specific enough to win, broad enough to grow

"Health" is too broad. "Type 2 diabetes management for women over 50" is just right. "Type 2 diabetes management for women aged 47 in Wyoming" is too narrow.

Test: Can you list 10 article topics or video ideas you'd make in this niche, off the top of your head? If yes, pass.

4. Audience that's actively searching for help

People are Googling, watching YouTube, or signing up for newsletters in this niche. There's demand.

Test: Type 3 of your potential niche topics into Google. Are there existing articles, blogs, channels, or tools? If yes, pass.

The 5 niche categories that work in 2026

Here are the categories where ordinary people consistently build affiliate income.

Health and wellness for specific demographics

Examples:

  • Pre-diabetes management for adults over 50
  • Joint health for active retirees
  • Sleep optimization for shift workers
  • Natural remedies for menopause symptoms

Why it works: specific demographic + recurring purchases + emotional buyers.

Hobbies with gear

Examples:

  • Beginner woodworking with hand tools
  • Backyard birdwatching for retirees
  • Home espresso for coffee snobs
  • Knitting for beginners

Why it works: hobbyists love their tools. They'll buy upgrades for years.

Practical home stuff

Examples:

  • Cooking for one or two people
  • Home gardening in small spaces
  • Decluttering for empty-nesters
  • Organizing the family kitchen

Why it works: broad audience, products everywhere.

Travel for specific groups

Examples:

  • Senior travel insurance
  • Slow travel for retirees
  • RV living for over-55s
  • Cruise tips for first-timers

Why it works: high-priced products, long cookie windows, lots of partners.

Career and finance for late-career adults

Examples:

  • Bookkeeping software for solopreneurs over 50
  • Phased retirement planning
  • Side hustles for empty-nesters
  • Online courses for career changers

Why it works: software products often pay $50 to $200+ per signup.

What to AVOID

A few niches we steer beginners away from:

Crypto and forex

Volatile, hype-driven, lots of fraud. Audience is hostile to your authentic voice.

Get-rich-quick / make-money-online

Saturated, untrustworthy, hard to compete unless you have a unique angle.

Adult / gambling / "gray area"

Affiliate networks ban these. Not worth the risk.

Anything you don't actually care about

You'll be writing about this for 2 years minimum. Pick something you can stand.

Your 30-minute niche selection exercise

Open a Google Doc. Title it "Niche Selection."

Step 1: Brainstorm 5 to 10 candidate niches (10 min)

Use these prompts:

  • What hobbies have I done for 5+ years?
  • What advice do friends ask me for?
  • What are 3 things I've recently bought online and researched extensively?
  • What's a problem I've solved that other people haven't?

Don't filter yet. Just list.

Step 2: Run the 4-test filter (15 min)

For each candidate, ask:

  • Genuine interest? (yes/no)
  • Commercial activity? (yes/no)
  • Specific enough? (yes/no)
  • Active audience? (yes/no)

Cross out anything that's not all 4 yeses.

Step 3: Rank the survivors (5 min)

Of the niches that survived, rank them 1 to 3 in order of "I'd genuinely enjoy doing this."

The #1 ranked niche is your niche. Commit to it.

How to research demand quickly

Once you've picked a niche, do this 15-minute sanity check:

1. Google Trends

Go to trends.google.com. Type your niche.

  • Trending up over 5 years? Good.
  • Stable? Fine.
  • Declining? Pick something else.

2. Amazon

Search your niche on Amazon. Look at:

  • How many products exist (you want 50+)
  • Average review counts (you want 100+ on top products - shows real buying)
  • Price ranges (you want $20+ - lower commissions on cheap items)

3. YouTube

Search your niche on YouTube. Look at:

  • Channels in this niche with 5,000+ subscribers
  • Recent video upload dates (channels still active = healthy niche)
  • View counts on top videos (10,000+ = real audience)

4. Pinterest

Search your niche on Pinterest. Look for:

  • Recent pins, lots of saves
  • Specific product roundups
  • Audience seems engaged

If 3 out of 4 of these check out - your niche is good. Commit.

Your homework before Module 4

Before you click "Mark as Complete," please:

  • Have brainstormed at least 5 candidate niches
  • Have run the 4-test filter on each
  • Have picked ONE
  • Have done the 15-minute demand sanity check
  • Have written it down: "My niche is: ____. I will not change it for 6 months."

If you're not 100% sure - that's okay. Pick the one you'd most enjoy. You can refine later if needed, but don't keep switching.

If you got stuck

"I can't pick between two niches"

Default to the one with HIGHER commercial activity (more products, higher prices). You can always do the other one as a future project.

"Everything I'm interested in seems too saturated"

Saturation is good - it means there's money. Your job isn't to pick an empty niche; it's to find a unique angle WITHIN a busy niche.

Example: "espresso machines" is saturated. "Espresso machines for small kitchens (under 6 inches of counter space)" is a unique angle.

"I picked a niche but I'm second-guessing"

Normal. Stick with it for at least 90 days. If at day 90 you genuinely hate it, pivot. But most people who pivot at day 30 just keep pivoting forever and never make money.
