Welcome back

Content without traffic is a journal. Traffic is what makes affiliate marketing work.

The good news: there are 5 reliable, ethical ways to get traffic. Once you pick 2 of them and do them consistently, your traffic will grow over time.

The bad news: it's slow. Faster than 2020. Slower than people promise you.

By the end of this module, you'll have a weekly traffic routine you can stick with.

The 5 traffic sources that work

1. Google search (SEO)

People Google your topic. Your article ranks. They click.

Pros: Free, compounds forever, works while you sleep.

Cons: Slow (6+ months minimum). Google has been harder since 2024 algorithm updates.

2. Pinterest

You create "pins" (visual graphics) linked to your articles. People save and click.

Pros: Faster than Google (3 months to start working). Friendly to over-50 demographics. Visual.

Cons: Requires making graphics (free with Canva, but takes time).

3. Facebook groups

You join groups in your niche. Comment helpfully. Share content occasionally (carefully).

Pros: Fast (immediate traffic possible). Real audience.

Cons: Can't be spammy. Most groups ban affiliate links. Requires real engagement.

4. YouTube (if you also have a YouTube channel)

Your videos drive traffic to your blog (or directly via description links).

Pros: YouTube algorithm is friendly to niche channels.

Cons: Requires being on camera (or AI avatars).

5. Email newsletter

Once you have a list, you email links to your new content.

Pros: Highest-trust traffic. Best conversion rates.

Cons: You have to build the list first.

Pick 2 (not 5)

Beginners try all 5 and burn out. Pick 2 to start.

For most beginners, the best combo is:

  • SEO + Pinterest (if you have a content site)
  • Facebook groups + Email (if you have an existing audience)
  • YouTube + SEO (if you're doing the YouTube path)

You can layer in more later. For your first 6 months: just 2.

SEO basics for beginners

You don't need to master SEO. You need to do 3 things consistently.

1. Pick keywords people actually search

Open Google. Type the first half of your topic. Look at autocomplete suggestions.

Those are real searches. Use one of them as your article title.

2. Match search intent

If someone Googles "best cookie sheets for thin cookies," they want a comparison article - not your life story.

Your article should answer the search query within the first 200 words. Lead with the answer, then add context.

3. Internal linking

Every new article you write should link to 2 to 3 of your older articles (where relevant). And your older articles should link to the new one.

Why: Google uses these links to understand which articles are most important on your site.

Realistic SEO timeline

  • Month 1-3: Article published. Google might start indexing.
  • Month 3-6: Article gets a few visitors per day.
  • Month 6-12: If the article is good, traffic starts compounding. 50+ visitors per day is realistic.
  • Year 2: Best articles get 200-1,000+ visitors per day.

This is normal. Don't quit at month 3.

Pinterest, walked through

Pinterest is the secret weapon for our age group. The audience is mostly women 30-65 - your demographic.

Setup

  1. Go to pinterest.com -> Sign up (free)
  2. Convert to a Business Account in settings
  3. Set your profile name to match your blog name
  4. Upload your blog's logo or a relevant photo

Creating pins

  1. Go to canva.com (free, simple design tool)
  2. Search "Pinterest pin templates"
  3. Pick one in your niche style
  4. Edit:
    • Add the title of your article
    • Add a hook (e.g., "5 cookie sheets we tested" or "The one nobody talks about")
    • Add your blog name
  5. Download as PNG
  6. Upload to Pinterest with:
    • Title: same as your article title
    • Description: 100-200 words including the topic
    • Link: your article URL
    • Board: a board you've created in your niche

Frequency

For each new article, make 3 to 5 different pins (different titles, different graphics).

Post 5 to 10 pins per week to grow. Once you have 30+ pins, traffic starts compounding.

Realistic Pinterest timeline

  • Month 1-2: No traffic yet. Building pin volume.
  • Month 3-4: First 100 visitors per month.
  • Month 6: 1,000+ visitors per month if you've been consistent.
  • Year 1+: 10,000+ visitors per month from Pinterest is normal.

Facebook groups, walked through

This is the fastest traffic source - if you do it ethically.

The 4-week patience play

Week 1-2:

  • Join 5 Facebook groups in your niche (ones with 5,000+ active members)
  • Read posts daily
  • Comment helpfully on 2-3 posts per day
  • Don't share your content yet. Build credibility first.

Week 3:

  • Continue commenting helpfully
  • Look for posts that ask questions your article answers
  • When relevant, mention "I wrote about this - here's the article" and share the link
  • Only do this when it's actually helpful - not as a promotion

Week 4+:

  • Share new articles when relevant (1 to 2 per week max)
  • Continue helpful commenting (don't only post links)

What NOT to do

  • Don't blast affiliate links directly in groups (instant ban from most)
  • Don't share the same article in 5 groups in the same hour (looks spammy)
  • Don't argue with people in comments (kills your reputation fast)

Realistic timeline

You can get your first 100 visitors from Facebook groups in your first month if you're active. The trick is being genuinely helpful.

Building an email list

Even if email is your secondary channel, build a list anyway. Lists make every other channel work better.

The minimum viable setup

  1. Sign up for MailerLite (free under 1,000 subscribers) at mailerlite.com
  2. Create one "lead magnet" - a free guide or checklist relevant to your niche
    • Example: "10 Best Cookie Sheets I Tested - PDF"
    • Make it in Google Docs, export as PDF
  3. Add a sign-up form to your blog: "Get my free guide"

What to email

  1. Welcome email (immediate after signup): deliver the free guide + introduce yourself
  2. Weekly email (every Tuesday or Thursday): share one helpful tip from your niche, link to a relevant article on your site
  3. Monthly email (last week of month): review what you've published, recommend 2 to 3 specific things

Realistic timeline

  • Month 1-3: 0 to 50 subscribers
  • Month 6: 100 to 500 subscribers
  • Year 1: 500 to 2,000 subscribers
  • Year 2: 2,000 to 10,000 subscribers (depending on niche)

A 1,000-person engaged list is worth $500 to $2,000/month. Worth the wait.

Your weekly traffic routine

Once you have content, build this routine. Total time: 5 to 8 hours per week.

Monday (1 hour)

  • Check analytics from last week
  • Note which posts got the most traffic
  • Plan this week's new content

Tuesday (2 to 3 hours)

  • Write or finalize one new article (with AI help)
  • Edit, add images, internal links

Wednesday (1 hour)

  • Make 3 to 5 Pinterest pins for the new article
  • Schedule them across the week

Thursday (1 hour)

  • Share new article in 1 to 2 Facebook groups (if it fits)
  • Send weekly email to your list (if you have one yet)

Friday (30 min)

  • Engage with comments on your articles
  • Reply to email subscribers
  • Comment helpfully in groups

Weekend

  • OFF. Rest is part of the work.

If you got stuck

"I'm overwhelmed by all of this"

Pick the simplest combo and do nothing else for 30 days:

  • 1 article per week (write Mon-Tue, polish Wed)
  • 3 Pinterest pins per article (make Wed-Thu, schedule Fri)

That's it. Two channels, one rhythm. Repeat for 30 days. Then evaluate.

"I'm not getting any traffic"

How long has it been? If less than 90 days - keep going. SEO is slow.

If more than 90 days and STILL no traffic, possible fixes:

  • Article titles don't match real search queries (run Module 6's "Google autocomplete" check)
  • Article quality is too thin (most affiliate articles need 1,500+ words)
  • No external sharing yet (Pinterest, Facebook groups, email)

"Pinterest seems hard"

It IS hard for the first month. You're learning what works. Make 30 pins before you judge.

"Facebook groups keep removing my links"

Read each group's rules. Some forbid all links. Others allow them in specific contexts.

The safer path: share useful comments first, get known by the admins, then occasionally link to your own content when it's specifically relevant.
