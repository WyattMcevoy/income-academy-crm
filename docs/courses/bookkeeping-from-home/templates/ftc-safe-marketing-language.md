# FTC-Safe Marketing Language for Bookkeepers

Phrases to use and phrases to avoid when marketing your bookkeeping services. Applies to website copy, email outreach, social media posts, ads, and in-person networking.

---

## Phrases that are generally safe

### About your service

✅ "I help small businesses keep their books current and tax-ready."

✅ "I provide monthly reconciliation, categorization, and financial reports."

✅ "I work with 3-5 clients at a time so each gets focused attention."

✅ "My clients generally save 5-10 hours per month they'd otherwise spend on bookkeeping."

✅ "You'll know where your business stands every month in plain English."

### About outcomes (with caveats)

✅ "Clean monthly books mean fewer tax prep surprises for you and your CPA."

✅ "Accurate financials help you make better business decisions — but they don't make those decisions for you."

✅ "I can't promise specific business outcomes because those depend on many factors outside my scope."

### About yourself

✅ "I'm a QuickBooks Online ProAdvisor" (if you have the free cert)

✅ "I've been bookkeeping for [X] years" (if true)

✅ "I carry Errors & Omissions insurance" (if you do)

---

## Phrases to avoid

### Anything implying CPA or tax authority

❌ "I'll save you money on taxes" (you can't make tax claims if you're not a CPA/EA)

❌ "Tax preparation and bookkeeping" (unless you're actually a CPA/EA, don't bundle these)

❌ "IRS representation" (this requires CPA/EA/attorney credentials)

❌ "Full-service accounting" (implies CPA-level services)

❌ "I'll advise on your best tax strategy" (you can't — that's tax advice)

### Guaranteed outcomes

❌ "Guaranteed to save you $X in taxes" (you can't guarantee tax outcomes)

❌ "You'll never worry about your finances again" (overpromising)

❌ "100% error-free bookkeeping" (errors happen; no one is 100%)

❌ "Perfect compliance, every time" (implies a guarantee you can't make)

### Income / earnings implications for clients

❌ "My clients grow revenue 30% after engaging me" (post hoc ergo propter hoc — you didn't cause the growth)

❌ "Work with me and increase your profit by $X" (not a claim you can substantiate)

❌ Testimonials that quote specific dollar earnings without disclaiming "results may vary"

### Misleading credentials

❌ "Certified" (unless you specify what — "Certified QuickBooks ProAdvisor" is fine; "Certified Bookkeeper" is vague and may be misleading)

❌ "Accountant" (unless you have an accounting degree AND are using the term in the generic sense — in some states "accountant" implies CPA)

❌ "I work with CPAs" (vague — if you have specific referral relationships, say that; otherwise skip)

## Testimonials — use carefully

### Safe to use

✅ Quotes about your service quality, responsiveness, clarity

Example: "Jane is responsive, her reports are easy to understand, and she actually reads my emails. Worth every penny."

✅ Quotes about what the client got back (time, peace of mind, better understanding)

Example: "I used to spend my Sundays reconciling books. Now I don't. That's the value."

### Use with disclaimer

⚠️ Quotes that mention income / savings numbers

Example: "Working with John saved me about $2,400 last year on [X]."

→ Requires disclaimer: "Results vary. [Client name] is an individual case and may not reflect typical outcomes. Your results depend on your business, market conditions, and factors outside our control."

### Avoid entirely

❌ Testimonials from clients who also had a significant change (new hire, new product launch, market shift) and attribute outcomes to your service alone

❌ Testimonials that imply you provided tax advice, legal advice, or business advisory you're not licensed to provide

❌ Paid testimonials without clear disclosure

## Website footer language (always include)

```
[YOUR BUSINESS NAME] provides bookkeeping services for small
businesses. We are not a CPA firm, attorney, or financial advisor.
The information provided on this website is for general educational
purposes only and should not be considered professional tax, legal,
or financial advice. Please consult your CPA or attorney for advice
specific to your situation.
```

## Ad-specific safety

For Facebook, Google, LinkedIn ads:

- Don't use images of money stacks, IRS letters, or "bank vault" imagery
- Don't use urgency language ("Last chance!", "Only 3 spots left!")
- Don't imply guaranteed outcomes ("Get your books perfect in 30 days!")
- Don't use before/after imagery implying financial transformation

Ad platforms are increasingly strict on financial-services ads. When in doubt, have your attorney or a marketing compliance consultant review before spending money.

---

## When to consult an attorney

- Writing your engagement letter (one-time, $200-$500 for review)
- Structuring testimonials with income claims
- Any marketing campaign budget over ~$5,000 (worth the $500 review fee)
- Before offering any guarantee ("guaranteed 30-day cleanup" type offers)
- State-specific licensing questions (NY, CA, TX have more rules)

Attorneys don't need to write your marketing — but a 30-minute review of your website + templates every 1-2 years catches things that would otherwise blow up on you.
