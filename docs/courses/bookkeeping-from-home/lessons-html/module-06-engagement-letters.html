Module 6: Engagement Letters and Legal Boundaries

Time: about 30 minutes What you'll do: Create a written engagement letter template you'll use with every new client.

Welcome back

This is the unglamorous but essential module.

An engagement letter is the written agreement between you and your client. It defines:

  • What you'll do (and what you won't)
  • How much you charge
  • How payment works
  • What happens if either of you wants out

Without an engagement letter, you have no protection. Disputes go to the client's interpretation. Scope creeps endlessly. You stay up at night wondering if you're going to get paid.

By the end of this module, you'll have a template ready to use.

Why you NEED an engagement letter

Three reasons.

Reason 1: Sets clear expectations

The biggest source of client conflict is mismatched expectations.

Engagement letter = both sides know exactly what's promised. No surprises.

Reason 2: Protects you from scope creep

"Can you also do X?" - if it's not in the engagement letter, it's a separate project at a separate price.

The letter is your shield against "while you're at it" requests.

Reason 3: Creates a paper trail

If you ever have a dispute, the engagement letter is the agreement. Courts (or arbitrators) look at this first.

Email-only agreements are also enforceable, but a formal letter is much stronger.

The template

Use this for every new client. Customize the bracketed parts.

ENGAGEMENT LETTER

Date: [date]

Bookkeeper: [Your Name]
[Your business name, if applicable]
[Your address]
[Your phone]
[Your email]

Client: [Client business name]
[Client name and title]
[Client address]
[Client phone]
[Client email]

---

SERVICES PROVIDED

The Bookkeeper agrees to provide the following services to the
Client:

1. [Service - be specific. e.g., "Monthly categorization of
   transactions in QuickBooks Online"]
2. [Service - e.g., "Monthly reconciliation of all bank and
   credit card accounts"]
3. [Service - e.g., "Monthly P&L and Balance Sheet reports"]

The Bookkeeper does NOT provide:
- Tax preparation or tax advice
- Financial planning or investment advice
- Audit or attestation services
- Anything outside the scope listed above

---

FEES

Monthly retainer: $[amount] per month
Billed: [Net 7 / Net 15 days from invoice / On the 1st of each month]
Payment methods: [Bank transfer, Stripe, etc.]

Late payment: 1.5% per month interest on past-due balances after 15 days

---

TERM AND TERMINATION

This agreement begins on [start date].

Either party may terminate with 30 days written notice. The
Client is responsible for fees up to the termination date.

---

SCOPE OF WORK

The services above are based on:
- [Approximate transaction volume]
- [Number of accounts]
- [Specific software]
- [Frequency of communication]

If the volume of work materially changes (e.g., transactions
double), the Bookkeeper reserves the right to renegotiate the fee.

Out-of-scope work (anything beyond the services listed above):
- Charged at $[hourly rate] per hour
- Billed monthly with detailed time logs
- Pre-approved by the Client for projects over [number] hours

---

CONFIDENTIALITY

The Bookkeeper will keep all Client financial information confidential.
The Bookkeeper will not share information with third parties without
the Client's written consent, except as required by law.

---

CLIENT RESPONSIBILITIES

The Client is responsible for:
- Providing access to financial accounts
- Sending receipts and documents promptly
- Reviewing and approving the monthly reports
- Making the final decisions about their books

The Client understands that the Bookkeeper is not a CPA and does
not provide tax advice. Tax-related questions should be directed
to the Client's CPA.

---

DISPUTE RESOLUTION

Any disputes will first be resolved through direct conversation.
If unresolved, both parties agree to mediation before litigation.
Governing law: [your state].

---

Bookkeeper:

___________________________

How to send it

Step 1: Create a clean Word doc or PDF

Use the template. Customize for the specific client.

Step 2: Send via email

Subject: Engagement letter for our work together

Hi [name],

Attached is the engagement letter for our bookkeeping services.

A few quick notes:
- The rate matches what we discussed: $[X]/month
- I've outlined exactly what's included (and what's not)
- Either of us can terminate with 30 days notice

If everything looks right, please sign and email it back. I'll
get started [start date].

Any questions, just reply.

[Your name]

Step 3: Get it signed

Most clients sign immediately. If they don't sign within a week, follow up:

Hi [name], just floating this back up. Easy 2-minute task to
get rolling. Let me know if you have any questions.

Step 4: Both sign and save

You both sign. You save the signed copy. You start work.

Don't start work without a signed engagement letter. It's tempting in your first few months. Don't do it. This is how you get burned.

Common engagement letter mistakes

Mistake 1: Too vague

"Bookkeeping services" doesn't define anything. The client thinks you'll do everything. You think you'll do basics.

Fix: List specific deliverables.

Mistake 2: No "what's not included" section

Without this section, the client assumes everything is included.

Fix: Explicitly list what's NOT included (especially tax advice).

Mistake 3: No termination clause

Some clients try to lock you in indefinitely. Or you might want out.

Fix: 30-day notice clause for both sides.

Mistake 4: No scope creep protection

"Can you also handle X?" leads to unpaid work over time.

Fix: Out-of-scope work clause with hourly rate.

Mistake 5: No "tax disclaimer" language

Without explicit "we don't do tax advice" language, you can be drawn into giving opinions.

Fix: Explicit disclaimer in the Client Responsibilities section.

When to terminate a client

The engagement letter gives you the right to terminate with 30 days notice. Use it when needed.

Signs to terminate

  • Consistently late payments
  • Unrespectful or abusive communication
  • Constant scope creep
  • Books are a disaster and they won't pay catch-up rates
  • The work makes you dread Monday morning

How to terminate

Hi [name],

I'm writing to let you know I'll be ending our bookkeeping
arrangement effective [30 days from today].

I'll wrap up [specific deliverables] before then and provide
all files in a clean handoff.

Thanks for the time we've worked together. Best of luck.

[Your name]

That's it. Don't justify. Don't argue. Just exit.

If you got stuck

"I'm scared to send a formal letter - feels too official"

It's official because it's professional. Clients EXPECT this from professional bookkeepers.

If anything, NOT sending one looks unprofessional.

"Should I have a lawyer review my template?"

Once you've used the template with 3 to 5 clients, yes - have a small business attorney review it for $200 to $500. Worth it.

For your first 3 clients, the template above is good enough.

"What if a client refuses to sign?"

That's a red flag about the client. Don't start work.

If they're already trying to skip the agreement, they'll be even harder to manage once you've started.
