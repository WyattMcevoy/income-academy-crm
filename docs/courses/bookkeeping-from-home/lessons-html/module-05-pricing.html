Module 5: Pricing That Doesn't Burn You Out

Time: about 30 minutes What you'll do: Build a pricing structure that pays you fairly without burning out.

Welcome back

Pricing is the #1 thing new bookkeepers get wrong.

Most beginners undercharge. They take on too much work for too little money, then burn out within 6 months.

By the end of this module:

  • You'll know how to price each of the 3 main models (hourly, per-project, retainer)
  • You'll know how to charge enough to be sustainable
  • You'll have a script for quoting on calls
  • You'll know when to walk away

The 3 pricing models

Model 1: Hourly

When to use: Catch-up bookkeeping (Service 2). Project-based work where you don't know exactly how long it'll take.

Range: $25 to $60 per hour for new bookkeepers. $60 to $120 for established ProAdvisors.

Pros: Honest. Easy to start.

Cons: Caps your income. You only earn while working.

Model 2: Project-based

When to use: Large catch-up jobs. 1099 batch processing. Anything with a clear scope.

Quote it: Estimate hours, multiply by hourly rate, add 30% buffer.

Example: 8 months of catch-up bookkeeping = ~25 hours at $40/hour + 30% buffer = $1,300 quoted.

Pros: Predictable income for both sides.

Cons: If you misjudge scope, you can lose money.

Model 3: Monthly retainer

When to use: Monthly bookkeeping (Service 1). The bread and butter.

Range: $300 to $1,200/month per client.

Pros: Predictable income. Strong client relationships. Less time selling.

Cons: Has to actually fit the work. If client volume balloons, you're stuck.

How to set your hourly rate

Your hourly rate has to cover:

  • Your time
  • Your software/tools costs
  • Your taxes (set aside 25-30%)
  • Slow weeks (some weeks are quieter than others)

The math

  1. What's the lowest annual income you'd accept? (For most students: $40,000 to $80,000 working 25 to 40 hours/week)

  2. Divide by 1,300 (25 hours/week, 52 weeks/year)

  3. Add 30% for taxes and slow weeks


Example:

  • Goal: $50,000/year working 25 hours/week
  • $50,000 / 1,300 = ~$38/hour
  • Plus 30% = $50/hour
  • Round to $50 to $55/hour as your rate

That's your floor. Don't go below it.

How to quote monthly retainer

The trick: price by complexity, not by hours.

If you charge $40/hour and tell a client "expect 5 hours of work per month" = $200/month - that's BARELY worth your time.

Instead:

"I charge a monthly retainer based on the complexity of the
books. For a small business with 100 to 300 transactions per
month, my rate is $400 to $700 per month. After I see your
books, I can give you a specific number."

Notice:

  • You set the floor at $400 ($400 / 5 hours = $80/hour effectively)
  • You don't quote hours - you quote outcomes
  • You leave room to go higher

Pricing tiers for monthly retainer

A simple framework:

Tier 1 (light): Up to 100 transactions/month. $300 to $500/month.

Tier 2 (medium): 100 to 300 transactions/month. $500 to $800/month.

Tier 3 (heavy): 300 to 1,000 transactions/month. $800 to $1,500/month.

Tier 4 (very complex): Multi-state, employees, inventory. $1,500 to $3,000+/month.

How to actually quote a price

The script

When a prospect asks "how much do you charge?":

"For monthly bookkeeping in your situation, I'd be in the
[X] to [Y] range per month. Where it lands depends on transaction
volume and a few specific factors. Want me to send a specific
quote after seeing the books?"

What this does

  • Gives a range (avoids sticker shock from a single big number)
  • Names the variable (transaction volume) - reasonable
  • Offers next step (specific quote)
  • Doesn't apologize

When they push back on price

Use this exact phrasing:

"I understand. My rate reflects the fact that I [list 2 specific
things you do]. I'd be happy to scope down to a smaller monthly
package if you'd prefer."

Examples of "specific things you do":

  • "I'm a Certified ProAdvisor"
  • "I reconcile every account monthly"
  • "I generate a custom monthly report you can share with your CPA"
  • "I respond to your messages within 24 hours during business days"

You're not negotiating - you're explaining VALUE.

When to walk away

Some clients aren't worth the price they're willing to pay.

Signs to walk away

  • They want full bookkeeping for $200/month and won't budge (you'll lose money)
  • They want you to handle "everything" but don't want to pay enough to cover it
  • They're disrespectful in initial conversations (it gets worse later)
  • Their books are a disaster AND they're cheap (the catch-up alone will exhaust you)

How to walk politely

"Thanks for considering me. I think we might not be the right fit
on price for this. If your situation changes, please reach out.
Otherwise, best of luck."

It's hard the first time. Easier the fifth time.

Cheap clients are usually difficult clients. Walk away.

Raising rates with existing clients

Eventually you'll want to raise rates. Here's how.

Once a year

Send this email to all clients on January 1:

Hi [name],

Wanted to give you 60 days notice: starting March 1, my monthly
rate is increasing to $[new rate].

This reflects [reason - e.g., "additional reconciliation services
I'm now including"]. Working with you has been a pleasure and I'd
love to continue at the new rate. If you need to make changes, I
understand.

Either way, thanks for the trust.

[Your name]

What to expect

  • 80% accept the new rate
  • 10% try to negotiate (offer to lock old rate for 6 more months)
  • 10% leave

That's normal. Replace the 10% with new clients at the new rate. You come out ahead.

If you got stuck

"I'm scared to charge $500/month - that feels like a lot"

It's not. CPAs charge $200 per HOUR. Bookkeeping at $500/month for ~5 hours of work = $100/hour. Reasonable.

The market supports these rates. Your prospects expect to pay them.

"What if a prospect insists on a lower rate?"

Two options:

  1. Scope down (less service for less money) - good for you both
  2. Walk away - they're cheap clients, not your customers

Don't lower your hourly value to keep a client. You'll resent the work.

"I don't know my rate yet - I'm starting"

For your first 2 to 3 clients, charge:

  • $40 to $50/hour for catch-up
  • $400 to $600/month for monthly bookkeeping

Once you have 3 clients, you'll know what feels right and you can raise from there.
