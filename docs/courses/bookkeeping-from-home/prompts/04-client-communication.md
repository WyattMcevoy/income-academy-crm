# Prompts — Client Communication

Emails, conversations, and handling awkward moments professionally.

## BK-30: Client hasn't sent requested info

```
I asked my client for [specific documents / access / clarification]
on [date, X days ago] and haven't received a response. I need it to
close their month.

Write me a polite follow-up email that:
- Reference the specific request + original date
- Explain why this delays their reports
- Offers a specific deadline (e.g., "if I don't have this by EOD
  Friday, the month-end report will be delayed to [revised date]")
- Doesn't scold or guilt-trip
- Leaves the relationship intact

Tone: professional, patient-but-clear. Clients sometimes need a
gentle nudge; they don't need a nanny.
```

## BK-31: Client is requesting something outside scope

```
My client [CLIENT NAME] asked me to do [TASK OUTSIDE OUR ENGAGEMENT —
e.g., "prepare their tax return" / "review a contract" / "give them
business advice on whether to buy a truck"].

Our engagement letter specifies scope as [WHAT'S IN-SCOPE].

Write a response that:
- Acknowledges the request (don't make them feel dumb for asking)
- Clearly explains this is outside our engagement + why
- Offers the right alternative (their CPA / an attorney / a
  consultant)
- Offers to help them prepare the right materials to bring to that
  other professional (which keeps me useful)
- If it's something I COULD add to the engagement, offers that as
  an option with a clear price

Tone: friendly boundary-setting. "Here's what I do, here's who to
go to for that, here's how I can help indirectly."
```

## BK-32: Polite no to a scope creep request

```
My client keeps adding small tasks to their monthly work that weren't
in our original engagement:
- [EXAMPLE 1 — "can you also track my mileage?"]
- [EXAMPLE 2 — "can you help me file my sales tax?"]
- [EXAMPLE 3 — "can you organize my receipts?"]

Each one is small. Collectively they're adding 3-4 hours/month to
work that's priced for 8 hours. I need to address this.

Write:
1. A conversation-starting email that raises the pattern without
   singling out any one request
2. My options to offer them (expand scope + raise price, OR keep
   scope tight + I continue rejecting new requests)
3. A specific pricing suggestion if we expand (e.g., "current
   $400/mo covers through X; adding Y and Z would make it $550/mo")

Tone: collaborative, not confrontational. This is a negotiation,
not an accusation.
```

## BK-33: Bad-news delivery — something went wrong

```
I made an error in [CLIENT]'s books: [SPECIFIC ERROR — e.g.,
"categorized 3 transactions wrong over 2 months, which made their
February P&L overstate office expenses by $1,200 and understate
profit by $1,200"]. I caught it during the March close.

Write me an email that:
- Leads with the error clearly (no burying)
- Explains the impact (what reports were affected, was anything
  already shared with their CPA?)
- Explains the fix and by when it'll be corrected
- Doesn't over-apologize (professional accountability, not groveling)
- Offers a remediation if appropriate (e.g., discount next month,
  extra audit on my next few closes)

Tone: adult accountability. "I made an error, here's the fix, here's
how I'll prevent it recurring." This builds trust more than pretending
nothing happens.
```

## BK-34: Client wants a rushed report before month-end

```
My client [CLIENT NAME] needs a financial report by [URGENT DATE
BEFORE NORMAL MONTH-END CLOSE]. Possible reasons: loan application,
investor meeting, unexpected tax situation, sale of business, etc.

Write two responses:

A) If I CAN accommodate:
   - Confirm the deadline I can hit
   - Explain any limitations (e.g., "I can generate a preliminary
     report with [caveats], or wait another [X days] for the final
     reconciled version")
   - Any additional fee for the rush work (standard rate or upcharge)
   - What I need from them urgently

B) If I CAN'T accommodate:
   - Explain why honestly (capacity, their data not ready, etc.)
   - Offer an alternative (preliminary report, referral to someone
     who can rush, modified deliverable)
   - Confirm the normal timeline I will hit

Tone: professional, solution-oriented. Urgency doesn't mean I drop
everything — it means I make a thoughtful call.
```
