## The monthly workflow per client

### Day 1-3 of each month: pull data

- Log into client's QuickBooks (or Xero)
- Connect/refresh bank feeds
- Pull credit card statements
- Note any new accounts the client opened

Time: 30 minutes per client

### Day 4-15: categorize

- Walk through each transaction
- Categorize correctly
- Flag anything unusual or unclear

Time: 1 to 4 hours depending on volume

### Day 16-20: reconcile

- Reconcile bank accounts to statements
- Reconcile credit cards to statements
- Investigate discrepancies

Time: 30 to 90 minutes

### Day 21-25: prepare reports

- Run P&L
- Run Balance Sheet
- Run any custom reports the client wants
- Write monthly summary email

Time: 30 to 60 minutes

### Day 25-30: deliver and communicate

- Email the client with reports + summary
- Answer client questions
- Schedule next month's work

Time: 30 minutes

**Total per client per month: 3 to 8 hours.**

For 5 clients = 15 to 40 hours per month. Sustainable for part-time work.

## Using AI to speed up the writing parts

AI doesn't categorize transactions for you. But it absolutely speeds up the COMMUNICATION around them.

### Use case 1: Drafting the monthly summary email

After running reports, you typically email the client a summary. AI can draft this.

### Prompt template

Open Claude. Use this:

```
You are a bookkeeper writing a monthly summary email to a small
business client.

Client name: [name]
Month: [month/year]

Key numbers:
- Revenue: $[X]
- Expenses: $[Y]
- Net: $[Z]

Notable items this month:
- [Bullet 1, e.g., "Software costs jumped 30% due to new tool"]
- [Bullet 2]
- [Bullet 3]

Questions for client:
- [Question 1, e.g., "Is the $400 charge from XYZ Corp deductible?"]
- [Question 2]

Tone: warm, professional, concise. Reports attached.

Length: 200 to 300 words.

Write the email.
```

You'll get a clean draft in 30 seconds. **Edit lightly, send.**

Time savings: 20 minutes per client per month.

### Use case 2: Categorization research

When you encounter an unfamiliar transaction:

```
I'm a bookkeeper categorizing a transaction. The merchant is
[merchant name]. The amount is $[amount]. The client runs a
[business type].

What's the most likely category for this transaction in
QuickBooks? Walk me through 2 to 3 options and which is most
likely.
```

This doesn't replace your judgment - but it helps when you're stumped.

### Use case 3: Drafting client onboarding documents

When you take on a new client, you typically send:

- Welcome email
- Document request list
- Access instructions

AI can draft all of these in your voice based on a few examples. **One-time setup, lifetime use.**

### Use case 4: Answering "tricky" client questions

Client emails: "Hey, my husband sometimes uses the business credit card for groceries. How should we handle that?"

You ask AI:

```
A client is using their business credit card for personal
expenses. As their bookkeeper, what's the cleanest way to
handle this so the books stay clean and the client understands
the tax implication for their CPA to handle?
```

Get a draft response. Edit, send.

## What AI CAN'T do

This is critical. Don't use AI for:

### 1. Actual judgment

"Should this be marketing or office supplies?" - that's your judgment based on context. AI doesn't know your client.

### 2. Verifying numbers

If you ask AI "does this reconcile?" - it'll guess. **You need to do the actual math.**

### 3. Tax advice

Don't ask AI for tax advice and pass it to clients. **You're not qualified, AI isn't qualified, your client deserves a CPA.**

### 4. Finding errors

AI can't audit books. It will pattern-match what looks reasonable. **Real bookkeeping requires actually reviewing the data.**

### 5. Replacing the engagement letter or formal docs

AI can DRAFT these. But you still need to read, edit, and own what you send.

## The onboarding checklist

When a client signs the engagement letter, you need to set up access. Use this checklist:

```
NEW CLIENT ONBOARDING - [Client Name]

Day 1:
[ ] Engagement letter signed and saved
[ ] Welcome email sent
[ ] Initial discovery call scheduled

Week 1:
[ ] Access to QuickBooks Online (Accountant access)
[ ] Access to bank feeds (or instructions to set up)
[ ] List of all accounts (banks, credit cards, loans)
[ ] Most recent month's statements (PDFs or screenshots)
[ ] Tax ID and entity type
[ ] Client's CPA's contact info (for hand-offs)
[ ] Communication preferences (email vs. text vs. monthly call)

Week 2:
[ ] Initial books review completed
[ ] Catch-up scope identified (if needed)
[ ] First monthly close on calendar
```

Walk through this with every client. **Don't skip steps.**

## Communicating with clients

Set expectations in the engagement letter and reinforce them in practice.

### Your standard communication policy

Add this to your welcome email:

```
COMMUNICATION POLICY:

I respond to messages within 24 hours during business hours
(Monday-Friday, 9am to 5pm [your timezone]).

For monthly bookkeeping work, expect:
- Reports by the [10th/15th/etc.] of each month
- A summary email from me within 24 hours of running reports
- A response to your questions within 24 hours

For urgent items (tax deadlines, audit requests), please call
or text - I prioritize these.

Outside business hours, I batch responses on the next business day.
```

This sets expectations and prevents 2am client texts.

### When clients ask for tax advice

You'll get this CONSTANTLY. The script:

```
"Great question, but it's outside what I do as your bookkeeper.
Your CPA is the right person. Want me to email them with the
context?"
```

Memorize this. Use it 10 times in your first 6 months.

## If you got stuck

### "I'm slow at categorizing transactions"

Practice. Spend 1 to 2 hours per week in the QuickBooks test drive account. **Speed comes with reps.**

By client 3 to 4, you'll be 2x faster than at client 1.

### "Some clients send me a chaos of receipts"

Set expectations: "Send all receipts via [system - e.g., Hubdoc, ReceiptBank, or even just emailed PDFs]."

Standardize, or you'll go crazy.

### "I'm spending way more time per client than I expected"

Two diagnostic questions:

1. Did you scope the client correctly? (Volume too high for retainer.)
2. Are you doing things outside scope? (Crossing into accounts payable when you only quoted bookkeeping.)

Adjust the engagement letter or bill out-of-scope hours.
