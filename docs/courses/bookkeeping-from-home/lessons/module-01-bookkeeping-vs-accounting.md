## Welcome back

The single biggest legal risk in this business is NOT understanding the line between bookkeeping, accounting, and tax preparation.

Cross the line accidentally and you risk:
- Being sued for "unauthorized practice"
- Giving advice that costs your client money
- Losing your client AND your reputation

This module makes the line crystal clear so you never accidentally cross it.

---

## The 4 distinct services

There are 4 separate professional services that small businesses buy. They're easy to confuse, but they're not the same.

### 1. Bookkeeping (this is YOU)

What it is: keeping accurate financial records.

Day-to-day work:
- Categorizing transactions
- Reconciling accounts (matching bank statements to books)
- Generating monthly reports (P&L, Balance Sheet)
- Invoicing customers
- Tracking accounts payable

**No license required in most states** - though some require basic registration.

### 2. Accounting

What it is: analyzing financial records, building forecasts, advising on financial decisions.

Day-to-day work:
- Reviewing books bookkeepers have prepared
- Building budgets and forecasts
- Advising on financial decisions
- Year-end financial statements

**Often requires CPA license** depending on state and what's specifically being done.

### 3. Tax preparation

What it is: filling out and filing tax returns.

Day-to-day work:
- Preparing federal tax returns
- Preparing state tax returns
- Advising on tax deductions and strategy
- Representing clients before the IRS

**Requires CPA, EA (Enrolled Agent), or specific tax preparer credentials** in most cases.

### 4. Financial advising

What it is: investment and financial planning advice.

Day-to-day work:
- Recommending investments
- Retirement planning
- Insurance recommendations

**REQUIRES specific licensing** (Series 7, CFP, etc.) - you cannot legally give this advice without it.

## What you can do (legally, as a bookkeeper)

You're qualified to do these without any special license:

**Day-to-day:**
- Categorize transactions in QuickBooks/Xero
- Reconcile bank and credit card accounts
- Track accounts receivable and payable
- Process invoices and bills
- Run payroll (if you complete a payroll-specific certification)
- Generate monthly P&L and Balance Sheet
- Track inventory (basic level)

**Reporting:**
- Provide monthly financial reports to clients
- Identify trends (e.g., "your software costs went up 30% this quarter")
- Flag unusual transactions for client review
- Prepare files for the client's CPA

**Communication:**
- Answer client questions about specific transactions
- Reconcile discrepancies
- Send invoice reminders to client's customers

---

## What you CANNOT do (without proper credentials)

These will get you in trouble:

**Tax advice:**
- "Is this deductible?" -> always answer: "That's a question for your CPA. I can flag it for them."
- "Should I take the home office deduction?" -> "Talk to your CPA - I can help organize the records they'd need."

**Financial advice:**
- "Should I take a loan from this lender?" -> not your area
- "Should I incorporate?" -> attorney/CPA territory
- "Should I take this contract?" -> business advice, not bookkeeping

**Audit / certification:**
- You cannot "certify" books as audited
- You cannot sign tax returns as preparer
- You cannot represent clients before the IRS

**The script** when these come up:

> "That's a great question, but it's outside what I do as your bookkeeper. Your CPA [or attorney, financial advisor, etc.] would be the right person. I'm happy to send them whatever records they need."

Memorize this.

## Why staying in your lane is good business

The line isn't just legal - it's strategic. Three reasons to STAY in your lane:

### Reason 1: Specialists earn more than generalists

A bookkeeper who's excellent at bookkeeping commands $40 to $60/hour. A bookkeeper who tries to also do tax advice for free... usually still charges $40/hour. **You don't earn more by doing more.**

### Reason 2: Referrals from CPAs

If you respect the line, CPAs LOVE referring you to their clients. They get a great bookkeeper to work with. You get a steady stream of referrals.

If you cross the line, CPAs see you as a competitor or threat. **They won't refer you.**

### Reason 3: Risk reduction

You can't be sued for things you didn't do. **Stay in your lane = stay safe.**

## What about handling tax-prep season?

Many bookkeepers DO support tax prep - in a specific, legal way:

You prepare the books cleanly throughout the year. At tax time, you:

1. Run reports (P&L, Balance Sheet, transaction lists)
2. Send these to the client's CPA
3. Answer the CPA's questions about specific transactions
4. Update the books based on year-end adjustments

**You're a key player on the team. You're just not the one filing the return.**

This is legal everywhere. It's also exactly what CPAs want.

## A real client conversation

Let me show you how this looks in practice. Imagine your client Sarah owns a small marketing agency.

### Wrong way (don't do this)

```
Sarah: "I'm thinking of buying a new computer for the business.
        Will it be tax deductible?"

You: "Yes, if it's used for business. You can probably deduct
      the full cost in the first year using Section 179."

Sarah: "Great, I'll go ahead and buy it then."
```

You just gave tax advice. If you're wrong, Sarah loses money. If she gets audited, she might say "my bookkeeper told me." Bad position.

### Right way

```
Sarah: "I'm thinking of buying a new computer for the business.
        Will it be tax deductible?"

You: "That's a great question for your CPA - they'll know exactly
      how to handle the deduction. If you go ahead and buy it, send
      me the receipt and I'll categorize it correctly. Your CPA can
      then optimize the tax treatment when they prepare your return."

Sarah: "Okay, I'll ask Mike at tax time. Thanks."
```

Same end result for Sarah. Zero legal risk for you.

## If you got stuck

### "What if my client doesn't have a CPA?"

That's actually common with very small businesses. Your answer:

```
"I focus specifically on bookkeeping. For tax questions, you'll
want to work with a CPA. Want me to recommend someone? I work
with [CPA name] - they specialize in small businesses like yours."
```

Build relationships with 2-3 local CPAs (Module 4 covers this).

### "I have a friend who's a CPA - can they tell me what to advise on?"

No. Even if your friend tells you "yes, that's deductible" - YOU don't say it to your client. Your friend can tell YOUR CLIENT. You just stay out of it.

### "What if I make a mistake about the line?"

Anyone can make a mistake. The fix:

1. Apologize: "I shouldn't have answered that - it's outside what I do"
2. Suggest: "Let's get [CPA] involved before you act on it"
3. Move on

Don't compound the mistake by hiding it.
