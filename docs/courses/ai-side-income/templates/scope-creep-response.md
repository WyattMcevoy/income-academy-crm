# Scope Creep Response — Declining "Just One More Thing"

How to say no to unscoped requests without damaging the relationship.

---

## The core principle

Every "could you also just..." is a negotiation. Your answer is one of three:
1. **Yes, it's already covered** (rare — check first)
2. **Yes, for an additional fee** (most common)
3. **No, this is out of scope** (for things that don't belong at all)

The mistake is saying "yes, sure" by default — which trains clients that your scope is infinite for the same price.

---

## Standard response template

```
Hi [NAME],

Happy to take a look at [REQUEST].

Quick context: [REQUEST] is outside the scope we agreed to in the proposal (which covered [BRIEF RECAP]). Adding it would take about [TIME ESTIMATE] and cost $[AMOUNT].

A few options:
- **Do it separately**: I can have it to you by [DATE] for $[AMOUNT]. Just reply "go" and I'll start.
- **Swap within current scope**: we could pause [CURRENT LOW-PRIORITY ITEM] to make room for this without additional cost.
- **Park it**: we note it as a future project and revisit at our next planning check-in.

Which works best for you?

[YOUR FIRST NAME]
```

---

## Short version (repeat request pattern)

```
Hi [NAME],

Want to check — [REQUEST] feels a bit outside our scope. Still happy to do it, just want to flag it.

Options:
- Swap for [CURRENT ITEM] this cycle (no cost change)
- Add as a separate $[X] project
- Add to our next planning call

What's best?

[YOUR FIRST NAME]
```

---

## Firm but kind (client has been pushing repeatedly)

```
Hi [NAME],

Picking up on this — I've noticed we've added a few things outside the original scope over the last [MONTHS]. Individually each one is small, but collectively they add up to about [N] hours/week beyond what we agreed to.

I love doing this work, and I don't want to keep saying "yes, I'll squeeze it in" and feel bitter about it later.

Two options to get us back on the same page:

**Option A**: Update our monthly scope to reflect what we're actually doing — $[NEW AMOUNT] covering [EXPANDED SCOPE].

**Option B**: Return to the original scope. Future additions get priced separately as we go.

Either one works for me. I'd rather we have this conversation explicitly than keep drifting.

Want to hop on a 15-min call to talk about it?

[YOUR FIRST NAME]
```

## For small asks (real small, like a question or 10-min thing)

Sometimes scope creep is "can you take a quick look at X?" — and the honest answer is yes, it's 5 minutes, just do it. The line I draw:

**Do it free if:**
- Under 15 minutes
- Happens less than 2x/month with this client
- Maintains the relationship you want

**Bill for it if:**
- Over 15 minutes
- Happens weekly
- Requires follow-up work or revisions
- The client is already marginal in the profitability column

The test isn't the task. It's the pattern.

## Pre-empting scope creep

### In the proposal / contract
- Include an explicit "Not included" section (see `templates/proposal-template.md`)
- State revision limits clearly
- Define what "project scope" means vs "additional work"

### In the kickoff call
- Walk through the "not included" list with them
- Example: "Just to be specific — social posting isn't part of this. Happy to help scope that separately later if you want."
- Gets them used to the concept before they need it

### In recurring check-ins
- Monthly: review what got delivered vs what was agreed
- Flag any scope drift gently
- "Heads up — we've been doing more [X] than planned. Want to formalize that or dial it back?"

## Mistakes to avoid

### "I'll try to squeeze it in"
No. Either it's in scope or it isn't. Squeezing means you're working for free and resenting it.

### "Let me know if you'd like me to do this for an extra fee"
Too passive. They will say "yeah sure," and then you deliver without a PO, and the month-end bill surprises them.

Better: Give a specific price + a specific turnaround + a specific "reply GO to start" prompt.

### Doing it first, billing later
Big trap. Never start work that wasn't in the original scope or isn't pre-approved.

### "Let me talk to [fake boss / accountant / wife]"
You're a professional. Your scope is your scope. No need to hide behind a fake gatekeeper.

---

## When scope creep signals a bigger problem

- Client consistently asks for 20%+ more than agreed: they under-budgeted or under-scoped
- Client sends bigger asks on Fridays at 5pm expecting Monday turnaround: they don't respect your time
- Client reacts with annoyance when you raise scope questions: power dynamic is off
- Client quietly adds items expecting you'll "handle it": they're testing boundaries

When 2+ of these show up, it's time for a come-to-Jesus conversation or an exit strategy.

---

## Key phrases to remember

- "Happy to do it as a separate project at $[X]"
- "We could swap [CURRENT ITEM] for this — same budget"
- "Let's park it for our next planning call"
- "That's outside what we agreed — want to explore what it'd take?"
- "I hear the request. Here's what I can do within our current arrangement..."

None of these say no. They all say "yes, with a boundary."
