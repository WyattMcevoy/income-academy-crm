The 4-Part Prompt Structure

Memorize this. It's going to be your friend forever.

ROLE + CONTEXT + TASK + FORMAT

That's the whole structure. Let me walk you through what each part means.

Part 1: ROLE

Tell the AI who it should pretend to be.

Bad: Write me a blog post about hydrangeas.

Good: You're a gardening writer with 10 years of experience writing for Better Homes & Gardens magazine.

Why it matters: When you tell the AI to play a role, it shifts the tone, vocabulary, and depth of what it produces. A "gardening writer for Better Homes & Gardens" writes very differently from a "scientific botanist" or a "chatty Instagram blogger." All three could write about hydrangeas, but they'd write completely different posts.

The trick: Make the role specific. "A writer" isn't enough. "A gardening writer with 10 years of experience writing for Better Homes & Gardens magazine" is enough.

Part 2: CONTEXT

Tell the AI the situation -- who the audience is, what's already known, what's been tried.

Bad: Write a blog post about hydrangeas.

Good: The audience is beginner gardeners in USDA Zone 7 who've never grown hydrangeas before. They're intimidated by the idea that hydrangea color depends on soil pH, so the post should reassure them that it's actually simple to manage.

Why it matters: Context tells the AI who you're writing FOR. Without context, the AI guesses. With context, it tailors. This is where the "specific" magic happens.

Part 3: TASK

Tell the AI exactly what you want it to do.

Bad: Write something about hydrangeas.

Good: Write a 500-word beginner-friendly blog post explaining the three most common types of hydrangeas (mophead, lacecap, paniculata) and which one is easiest for a first-timer to grow successfully.

Why it matters: "Something" gives you something. A specific task gives you exactly that thing. The more specific the task, the closer the output is to what you actually want.

Part 4: FORMAT

Tell the AI how the output should look.

Bad: Just write it.

Good: Use a warm, neighborly tone with one personal-experience story near the start. Include a section header for each type of hydrangea. End with a 3-step "what to do this weekend" action list.

Why it matters: Without format, you get a wall of text. With format, you get exactly the shape of output you needed.

Press Enter. Read what comes back carefully. It'll probably be:

  • Vague (no audience specified)
  • Boring (no specific personality or angle)
  • Generic (could be selling any vacuum to anyone)
  • Maybe too long

Notice your gut reaction. Would you actually send this email? Probably not.

Step 2: Try a GOOD prompt for the same task

Now in the same conversation, type this:

You're a friendly direct-response copywriter who specializes in selling household products to women aged 50-70.

The audience is people who own a 3-bedroom suburban home, have grandchildren who visit weekly, and are tired of cordless vacuums that lose suction halfway through the living room. They've been burned by overpriced "premium" brands and want something practical, durable, and well-priced.

Write a 200-word email selling the new "HomeMaster 5000" cordless vacuum. The vacuum has 60 minutes of battery life, a HEPA filter, and a 3-year warranty.

Use a warm, slightly humorous tone. Open with a relatable scenario about grandchildren tracking in dirt. Make sure the email feels like a friend recommending something, not a salesperson pitching. End with a soft call-to-action: "Try it for 30 days. Send it back if you don't love it."

Press Enter. Compare the two outputs.

The second one will be dramatically better. You did that. You took the same task and got 5x better output by giving the AI:

  • A specific role
  • A specific audience
  • A specific product
  • A specific tone and structure

Step 3: Notice what changed

Look at your two outputs side by side. The second one will:

  • Sound like a specific person, not a generic robot
  • Actually address the audience (mentions grandchildren, relatable scenarios)
  • Have a clear opening, middle, and end
  • Include the soft CTA you asked for

Two bonus techniques that take you to the next level

Once you've got the 4-part structure down, these two techniques will make you sound like an absolute pro.

Technique 1: "Few-shot" prompting (a fancy word for "show, don't just tell")

If you want the AI to write in a specific style, give it 1-2 examples of that style first.

Example:

You're a writer for [client name]'s LinkedIn page. Write in their voice.

Here are two examples of posts they've written before:

EXAMPLE 1:
"Just got back from coffee with a client who's been struggling with hiring. We talked about how the 'right person' rarely comes from a job board  --  they come from a referral, a comment they left on your blog, or a connection at a chamber event. Hiring isn't a transaction. It's a relationship."

EXAMPLE 2:
"Reading 'Atomic Habits' for the third time. The chapter on identity-based habits keeps hitting me differently. We don't change because we want to do something  --  we change because we want to BE someone. Worth the re-read."

Now write 5 new posts in this same voice about [topic].

What this does: gives the AI a "voice fingerprint." It'll write the new posts in the same style, with the same length, the same conversational tone, and the same depth as the examples.

This is how you write in a client's voice without ever needing to "match their tone" yourself. You just feed in their existing posts as examples.

Technique 2: Ask the AI to ask YOU questions first

Sometimes you don't know enough about your own task to write a good prompt. Here's the trick:

I want to write a blog post about [topic], but I don't know enough yet. Before you write anything, ask me 5 questions that would help you write a much better post.

Press Enter. The AI will reply with 5 specific questions. You answer them. Then the AI writes the post -- using your answers as the prompt.

This is brilliant for two reasons:

  1. The AI knows what info it needs better than you do
  2. It forces you to actually think about your topic before writing

Try it. It feels weird at first ("why am I being interviewed by my AI tool?"), but the output quality is dramatically better.

When to start over vs. when to refine

Here's a small skill that'll save you hours: knowing when to scrap an AI's output and start over vs. when to keep refining it.

Refine when:

  • The structure is right, but the tone is slightly off
  • It got 80% of what you wanted; you just need to tweak 20%
  • Your follow-up would be small ("make this paragraph shorter," "use a different example")

Start over when:

  • The AI completely misunderstood what you wanted
  • The tone is fundamentally wrong (too formal, too casual, too sales-y)
  • You've already given 3+ follow-ups and it's still not getting closer

Why this matters: AI conversations have a kind of "momentum." If you start with a bad prompt and try to fix it through follow-ups, the AI keeps trying to satisfy your earlier (bad) instructions. It's almost always faster to scrap and rewrite the prompt cleanly than to keep correcting.

Don't be afraid to start a new conversation. It costs nothing.

If you got stuck

"My 'good prompt' didn't seem that much better than my 'bad prompt'"

Two possibilities:

  1. Your good prompt wasn't specific enough. Add MORE detail: a specific year, a specific number of words, a specific tone, a specific example. The more specific, the better the output.

  2. The bad prompt got lucky. Some tasks are simple enough that even a bad prompt works. Try a more complex task -- like the vacuum email -- to see the dramatic difference.

"The AI keeps refusing to write in someone's specific voice"

This usually happens when you ask it to imitate a real public figure. Both Claude and ChatGPT are careful about this. Workaround: instead of saying "write like Donald Trump" or "write like Oprah," describe the voice traits ("write in a confident, plain-spoken, slightly hyperbolic style with frequent use of short emphatic sentences"). Same effect, no refusal.

"I tried the 'ask me 5 questions' trick and the questions were dumb"

Try Claude instead of ChatGPT, or rephrase: "Ask me 5 specific, useful questions that will help you write a better [thing]." If the questions are still bad, you might be asking about a topic the AI doesn't know well. Pick a different angle.

"How long should a good prompt be?"

There's no rule, but most professional prompts are 3-8 sentences. Anything shorter than 3 is probably missing one of the 4 parts (Role, Context, Task, Format). Anything longer than 8 is probably overkill. Aim for the middle.
