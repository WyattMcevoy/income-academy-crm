Module 7: Boundaries, Scheduling, and Not Burning Out

Time: about 20 minutes What you'll do: Set up working hours, learn how to say "no" gracefully, and protect this work from taking over your life.

Welcome back

Burnout is the #1 reason new freelancers quit.

You'll be fine for the first 30 days. Excited, energized, things working. Around day 60 - if you haven't set boundaries - you start dreading Monday. By day 90 you're working evenings and weekends to keep up. By month 4 you're done.

This module makes sure that doesn't happen to you.

The whole point of this work is to enrich your life, not consume it. That requires actual rules.

Set working hours

Pick a window. Stick to it.

Examples that work for our age group

  • "I work Tuesday to Friday, 9am to 1pm." (16 hours per week)
  • "I work weekday mornings only, 8am to noon." (20 hours per week)
  • "I work Monday, Wednesday, Friday, full days." (about 24 hours per week)

The exact schedule doesn't matter. What matters is that you have one and you communicate it to clients.

How to communicate it to clients

Add this to your email signature:

Working hours: Tuesday-Friday, 9am to 1pm Mountain Time
Outside those hours, I'll respond next business day.

Add this to your proposal template:

TURNAROUND: I respond to messages within 24 hours during my
working hours (M-F, 9am-1pm MT). Urgent same-day requests
add a $50 rush fee.

Why this works

  • Sets clear expectations from the start
  • The "rush fee" stops most "urgent" requests from actually being urgent
  • Clients respect freelancers who respect themselves

The "Inbox Zero" rule for client messages

This is the single biggest stress reducer.

Rule: check email twice per day. Once at the start of working hours, once at the end. Reply to everything in those two windows. Otherwise, don't open your inbox.

How to actually do this

  • Turn off email notifications on your phone (Settings -> Notifications -> Mail -> Off)
  • Turn off email notifications on your computer (System Settings -> Notifications -> Mail -> Off)
  • Set a timer for 30 minutes when you DO open email
  • Reply to everything that needs a reply
  • Close the tab when the timer goes off

That's it.

What about urgent messages?

Almost nothing is actually urgent. But if a client really does need you outside working hours:

  • They can pay the $50 rush fee
  • They can wait until next business day
  • That's it. Two options.

You'll be amazed how rarely anything is "actually urgent" once you charge for it.

Saying "no" gracefully

You'll get requests that don't fit. Here's how to say no without burning the relationship.

"Can you do this thing that's not what we agreed on?" (scope creep)

Happy to help with that - it's a separate project from what we
have set up. I can quote it as an add-on. Want me to send a
quick estimate?

This works because:

  • You said yes to helping
  • You said no to doing it for free
  • You offered the next step (quote)

"Can you start tomorrow?"

I'd love to. My calendar is full this week. The earliest I could
start is [date]. Should we lock that in?

This works because:

  • You set the timeline
  • You don't apologize
  • You offered a clear next step

"Can you do this for less?"

(Already covered in Module 6 - "I understand. My rate reflects [reason]. I'd be happy to scope down...")

"Can you do this kind of work?" (something outside your service)

That's outside what I focus on, but I have someone I trust who
specializes in it. Want me to make an introduction?

This works because:

  • You're honest
  • You stay helpful
  • The other freelancer remembers you referred and may refer back

Don't work weekends (yet)

For your first 6 months, this rule is sacred:

Weekends are for not working.

Why?

  • You'll burn out within 90 days if you work weekends
  • Clients who expect weekend work are bad clients
  • You need rest to do good work
  • Your family and friends matter more than any single project

If a client expects weekend work, gently push back:

Happy to take a look first thing Monday morning. Have a great
weekend!

Then close the laptop. Go for a walk.

The "skim test" for new client work

When a new client offer comes in, ask yourself these 4 questions:

  1. Will I genuinely enjoy doing this work?
  2. Does the rate hit my floor or higher?
  3. Does the client communicate respectfully?
  4. Does this fit within my working hours?

If any answer is "no," reconsider.

Two "no" answers = walk away.

You don't have to take every client. You're allowed to be selective.

The Sunday "next week" planning ritual

Every Sunday evening, 15 minutes:

  1. Look at next week's calendar
  2. List the deliverables due that week
  3. Block time for each one (Tuesday morning = blog post, Wednesday afternoon = newsletter, etc.)
  4. Note which clients owe you something (responses, payments, materials)

This single ritual prevents 80% of "I forgot a deadline" stress.

Some students use a paper notebook. Some use Google Calendar. Some use a simple Google Sheet. Whatever works for you - just make it consistent.

These are small habits. They're also the difference between a sustainable career and burnout.

If you got stuck

"I feel guilty taking time off when I just started"

Normal. Most people feel that way for the first 60 days.

Here's the truth: the work you do in week 4 is way better than the work you do in week 12 if you didn't rest. You're not lazy for having boundaries. You're protecting your career.

"My first client is a personal friend - boundaries feel awkward"

This is the hardest version of boundaries. Two tips:

  1. Set the rules in writing at the start ("Just so we're on the same page, I work Tu-F mornings...") - friends respect this once they see it's professional
  2. Charge them slightly LESS than full rate, but be firm about the hours - "friend rate is $30/hour, not free"

If your friend can't handle either of those, don't work for them. You'll lose the friendship faster than the client.

"A client expects 24/7 availability"

Time to have a hard conversation:

Hey [name], I want to revisit our working arrangement. I respond
to messages during business hours - Tuesday to Friday, 9am to 1pm.
I've been bending that rule and it's not sustainable.

Going forward, I'll batch all responses into those windows.
Urgent same-day requests will be billable as rush work at
[rate].

If that doesn't work, I understand and we can wrap things up
this month.

Then mean it. Most clients adjust. A few leave. Both are fine.
