Welcome back

Pricing is the thing that paralyzes most beginners.

The problem isn't usually "what's the right price." The problem is that we're not used to naming a price for our own work. It feels uncomfortable. That discomfort is exactly why most beginners undercharge - and then burn out.

By the end of this module, you'll know:

  1. How to figure out what your time is actually worth
  2. When to charge hourly, per project, or per month
  3. How to talk about money without flinching
  4. How to raise prices without scaring clients off
  5. When to fire a client (yes, it happens)

The goal: you make $1,500 to $3,500 per month doing work that doesn't burn you out. Pricing right is most of how you get there.

Step 1: What's your time actually worth?

Most beginners undercharge because they think: "I'm new, I shouldn't charge much."

That's backwards. Charge what the work is worth, not what you feel worthy of.

Here's the math:

What clients pay for

When a small business hires you to write blog posts, they're not paying for "1 hour of typing." They're paying to AVOID:

  • Spending their own 4 hours doing it themselves
  • Hiring an agency at $1,000 per post
  • Hiring an in-house writer at $50,000 per year
  • Doing nothing and continuing to lose customers to competitors

Their alternatives all cost MORE than you. You're a bargain at $250 per post, even if you only spend 2 hours on it.

Your real hourly value

Here's how to calculate what you should charge per hour:

  1. What's the lowest acceptable annual income for the work you'd want to do? (For most students: $30,000 to $60,000 working 20 hours per week)
  2. Divide by 1,000 (20 hours per week, 50 weeks per year)
  3. Add 30% to cover taxes, software, healthcare, slow weeks
  4. That's your hourly rate floor.

Example:

  • Goal: $40,000 per year working 20 hours per week
  • $40,000 / 1,000 = $40
  • Plus 30% = $52 per hour
  • Round up to $55 to $60 per hour.

Don't go below this. Ever.

Three ways to charge

Hourly

When to use: Work where the time required varies a lot, like email triage, bookkeeping support, or open-ended research.

How to quote it: "$25 to $40 per hour, billed weekly. I'll send a time log with the invoice."

Pros: Easy to start, fair to both parties. Cons: Caps your income (you only get paid for hours worked).

Per project

When to use: Work with a clear deliverable, like a blog post or a transcription job.

How to quote it: "$250 per blog post" or "$60 per audio hour."

Pros: Clients know the cost. You get rewarded for working efficiently. Cons: If the project drags, you can lose money.

Monthly retainer

When to use: Ongoing work for the same client, like LinkedIn ghostwriting or monthly newsletter writing.

How to quote it: "$600 per month for [specific deliverables]."

Pros: Predictable income. Clients commit to you. Less time selling, more time doing. Cons: Setting expectations is harder.

Which one for which service?

Service Best pricing model
Writing & editing Per project ($150 to $500 per piece)
Bookkeeping support Monthly retainer ($200 to $400)
LinkedIn ghostwriting Monthly retainer ($300 to $800)
Customer email triage Hourly ($15 to $25) or monthly retainer
Transcription Per audio hour ($40 to $80)

How to actually quote a price (without flinching)

The hardest part isn't deciding what to charge. It's saying it out loud.

The script

When asked "how much do you charge?", say:

"For this kind of project, I'd be in the range of [low end]
to [high end]. Where it lands depends on [the variable that
matters - length, complexity, etc.]. Want me to put together a
specific quote based on what you described?"

Why this works

  • You give a range (not an exact number) so they don't feel boxed in
  • You name the variable that determines price (so they understand)
  • You offer to follow up with specifics (controls next step)
  • You don't apologize. Don't say "is that okay?" or "I know it's a lot..."

What to do when they push back on price

The single most important sentence in pricing:

"I understand. My rate reflects [specific reason]. I'd be happy to scope down the project to fit your budget if you'd like."

Examples:

  • "I understand. My rate reflects the fact that I include 1 round of revisions and verify every fact before delivery. I'd be happy to scope down to a shorter post or fewer revisions to fit your budget."

  • "I understand. My rate is based on the time it takes to do this carefully. If the budget is firm, I could focus on just the highest-priority pieces - say 2 posts per month instead of 4."


You're not lowering the price. You're offering less work for less money.

When to walk away

If a client wants the same work for half the price, walk away politely:

"Thanks for considering me. I think we might not be the right
fit on price for this project. If that changes, please reach
out. Otherwise, best of luck."

This is hard the first time. Easier the fifth time.

Cheap clients are usually difficult clients. Walk away.

How to raise rates with existing clients

Eventually, you'll want to raise rates. Here's how to do it without losing them.

Once a year, on January 1st

Send this email at the start of the new year:

Hi [name],

Quick heads up - starting February 1st, my rates are increasing
to $[new rate] per [unit].

I wanted to give you 30 days notice so you have time to budget.
Working with you has been a pleasure and I'd love to keep
going at the new rate, but I understand if you need to make
changes.

Either way, thanks for the trust this past year.

[Your name]

Why this works

  • 30 days notice = professional
  • "I understand if you need to make changes" = no pressure
  • January 1st = standard time of year for rate changes, not awkward
  • 10 to 20% raises are normal - clients expect them

What to expect

  • 80% of clients accept the new rate
  • 10% try to negotiate (offer to lock the old rate for 3 more months, then raise)
  • 10% leave

That's normal. Replace the 10% who left with new clients at the new rate. You come out ahead.

When to fire a client

Yes, this happens. Some clients aren't worth keeping.

Signs you should fire a client

  • They consistently pay late or don't respect your invoicing
  • Every project becomes "scope creep" - they keep asking for more without paying more
  • They send abusive or disrespectful messages
  • The work makes you dread Monday morning
  • The hourly rate, when you actually calculate it, is below your floor

How to fire them politely

Hi [name],

I've been thinking about our working relationship and I think
it's time for me to step back. I'll wrap up [current project]
by [date] and won't be taking on more work after that.

Thanks for the time we worked together. Best of luck.

[Your name]

That's it. Don't explain. Don't justify. Just exit.

Why you should fire bad clients

Every hour spent on a bad client is an hour you can't spend on a good client. Bad clients block your career growth.

You'll feel guilty the first time. After that, you'll feel free.

If you got stuck

"I'm worried I'll charge too much and lose the client"

You're more likely to charge too LITTLE and resent the work.

Here's the truth: clients who balk at $40 per hour are the same clients who'll balk at $20 per hour and find another reason to pay late. Price is a filter. Higher prices attract better clients.

Start with the rate floor from earlier. Don't go below it.

"What if a client offers me less than my rate but I really need the money?"

If you're under financial pressure, take the work. One time. Then immediately go find more prospects so you don't have to take low-rate work next time.

Don't make it a pattern.

"How do I quote per project when I don't know how long it'll take?"

Three steps:

  1. Estimate hours honestly
  2. Multiply by your hourly rate
  3. Add 30% for contingency (things always take longer than you think)

That's your project price.

After 5 to 10 projects, you'll calibrate and stop needing the 30% buffer.
