The discovery call

Most beginners are terrified of the first call. Don't be. It's just a conversation.

Length: 15 minutes. Yes, really. 15 minutes is enough.

Goal: figure out if you can help, what they want, and what to charge.

The script (use this verbatim if you want)

1. Open warmly.

"Hi [name], thanks so much for taking the time. Should we keep this to about 15 minutes? I want to be respectful of your day."

2. Ask them to talk first.

"Tell me a bit about [their business] - what do you do day to day? What's been going well lately, what's been a pain?"

(Listen for 5 to 7 minutes. Don't interrupt. Take notes.)

3. Ask about the specific problem.

"You mentioned [the thing they want help with]. What does the current situation look like? How are you handling it now?"

4. Ask about the goal.

"If we worked together, what would success look like 60 days from now?"

5. Ask the budget question.

"Most folks I work with budget somewhere in the [range from Module 3] range per month for this kind of help. Does that fit what you've been thinking?"

6. Wrap up.

"Great. Let me put together a short proposal with what I'd do, the timeline, and the cost. I'll have it to you tomorrow. Sound good?"

Why this script works

  • You let them talk first (people love being heard)
  • You ask about the goal, not the task (better answers)
  • You name the price range (no awkward dance)
  • You commit to a follow-up (controls next step)

Right after the call

  • Take 5 minutes to write down everything you remember
  • Note what they said about budget, timeline, and tone
  • Save your notes - you'll use them in the proposal

Writing the proposal

A proposal is one page. Maybe two. Never more.

Use this template:

Hi [name],

Great talking today. Here's what I'd suggest:

WHAT I'D DO:
- [Specific deliverable 1]
- [Specific deliverable 2]
- [Specific deliverable 3]

TIMELINE:
- First draft of [thing] by [date]
- Revisions within [time]
- Ongoing delivery: [cadence]

COST:
- [Specific dollar amount]
- Billed [monthly / per project]
- First invoice due [date or terms]

WHAT'S NOT INCLUDED (so we're aligned):
- [Anything you're NOT doing - clarifies scope]

NEXT STEPS:
- If this looks right, just reply "let's do it"
- I'll send a quick contract for both of us to sign
- We'll get started [day]

Looking forward to it.

[Your name]

Why this proposal works

  • Specific deliverables - they know what they're getting
  • Specific timeline - they know when to expect things
  • Specific price - no confusion
  • "What's not included" - sets expectations and avoids scope creep
  • "Just reply 'let's do it'" - removes friction to say yes

Realistic close rates

  • 80% of proposals get a response within 5 days
  • About 50% of proposals get accepted
  • The other 50% either negotiate or pass

If they pass, ask why. Then improve the next one.

The full delivery workflow

This is the heart of the module. Walking you through a complete blog post for a chiropractor, end to end.

The same workflow applies to all 5 services - just substitute your specific deliverable.

Step 1: Get a clear brief from the client

Before writing anything, you need:

  • The exact topic
  • The audience (who they're trying to reach)
  • The tone (their voice)
  • The length
  • The goal (what should the reader do after reading?)

Send the client this exact intake email:

Hi [name], to make sure I nail the first draft, can you fill in:

1. Topic: [main subject]
2. Audience: [who reads your blog]
3. Tone: pick one - friendly / professional / playful / serious
4. Length: how long is "right" for your blog usually?
5. Goal: what should a reader do after reading? (e.g., book
   appointment, share post, just feel informed)
6. Anything to avoid? (topics, words, competitors)
7. One example of a past post I should match in style?

I'll have a first draft to you within 48 hours.

Step 2: Research with AI

Open Claude. Use this prompt:

You're a research assistant for a [chiropractor / contractor / etc.]
who writes a blog for [audience].

Topic: [topic from the brief]

Find me 8 to 10 recent (within last 2 years) findings, tips, or
angles on this topic that would be valuable to a [audience] reader.
Cite the source for each one.

Press Enter. Wait for the response.

Now verify every single citation. Open a new tab. Search for each cited source. If you can't find a source, ditch that point.

This takes 15 to 20 minutes. It's the most important step in the entire process.

Step 3: Outline with AI

Same conversation. Add:

Based on the verified findings above, give me a blog post outline
with:
- A hook headline
- An intro that addresses the reader's pain point
- 3 to 5 main sections, each with a header
- A conclusion with a clear call to action

Read the outline. Adjust anything that doesn't fit. If the structure is off, fix it now - it's much easier than fixing it after the draft.

Step 4: Draft with AI

Same conversation. Add:

Write a [length] blog post following this outline. The audience is
[audience]. The tone should be [tone]. Use simple language - aim
for an 8th-grade reading level. Include one specific story or
example near the start to hook the reader.

You'll get a draft.

Step 5: Edit ruthlessly

This is where you earn your money. Read the draft and:

  • Cut everything that's vague. "Many studies show" - which studies? Cut or replace.
  • Replace generic phrases with specific ones. "A lot of people" becomes "62% of new patients."
  • Add the human touch. A small joke, a personal observation, a "you know that feeling when...?"
  • Read it aloud. If it sounds like a robot, rewrite that sentence.
  • Spot-check facts. Even if Claude verified during research, double-check anything that sounds suspicious.

This pass takes 30 to 60 minutes. It's why your work is worth $250 per post and AI alone is worth $0.

Step 6: Polish

  • Run through Grammarly (free version is fine)
  • Check headings and formatting
  • Make sure links work
  • Verify the call to action is clear

Step 7: Deliver

Email the client:

Hi [name],

First draft of [post topic] attached.

Quick notes:
- Length: [word count]
- Reading level: [check it via free tools]
- Sources are linked inline - you'll want to verify [you, doctor]
  is comfortable with the medical claims before publishing
- Two angles I considered but didn't include: [list]. Happy to
  swap in if you'd prefer.

Let me know if you want any changes. I'll have a final ready
within 24 hours of your feedback.

[Your name]

Why this works

  • You named what's in the deliverable
  • You flagged things THEY need to verify (medical claims, in this case)
  • You showed the work behind the work (other angles you considered)
  • You committed to a fast revision turnaround

Quality control checklist

Before you hit "Send" on any deliverable:

  • Did I fact-check every specific claim?
  • Did I read it aloud once? Does it sound like a human?
  • Did I add at least one detail or example AI wouldn't have come up with?
  • Did I match the client's voice (or my best guess of it)?
  • Is anything in here a client could object to? (tone, accuracy, style)

If yes to all five, send it.

If no to any, fix it before sending.

Getting paid

Two methods that work for our age group:

Method 1: Use a simple invoicing tool

Send the invoice the same day you deliver the work. Net 7 days for new clients (don't extend longer until trust is built).

Method 2: Get paid through Upwork

If your client came from Upwork, all the payment handling is automatic. Set up direct deposit on the Upwork site - takes 5 minutes.

When clients are slow to pay

  • Day 7 (due date): polite reminder email
  • Day 14: more direct email
  • Day 21: pause work until paid

You have leverage. Use it kindly but firmly.

If you got stuck

"I had the call but they didn't reply to my proposal"

Send a friendly nudge after 5 days:

Hi [name], just floating this back up in case it got buried.
Happy to make any tweaks if it's useful. Either way, let me
know what you're thinking.

If no reply after that, move on. You'll find another.

"I delivered the work but the client didn't like it"

Two possibilities:

  1. The brief wasn't clear enough (your fault) - go back to step 1, get a better brief, redo
  2. The client doesn't actually know what they want - work with them to clarify

Either way, don't take it personally and don't get defensive. Say: "Thanks for the feedback. Let me redo with these notes in mind." Most "bad first drafts" become great second drafts.

"I'm scared the AI's work isn't good enough"

You're not delivering AI's work. You're delivering YOUR work, with AI's help. The 30 to 60 minutes you spend editing is the part the client is paying for.

The way you know it's good: would you publish this under your own name? If yes, send it. If no, edit more.

"I don't know what to charge for revisions"

Standard policy: 1 round of revisions included. Beyond that, $50 to $100 per round.

Put this in your proposal up front so it's never a surprise.
