What is ChatGPT? What is Claude?

Both ChatGPT and Claude are something called a Large Language Model, or LLM for short. That's the technical term, but the technical term doesn't really matter. What matters is what they actually do.

Here's the honest, plain-English version:

ChatGPT and Claude have read a huge amount of text from the internet, books, articles, and conversations. When you ask them a question, they're not really "thinking" the way you and I do. They're predicting the next most likely word, then the next, then the next -- based on everything they've ever read.

That's it. That's the whole magic trick.

Now, you might be wondering: "If they're just predicting words, why does it feel like I'm having a real conversation with a smart person?"

The answer: because they've read so much, their predictions are uncannily good. They've seen millions of how-to articles, so they can write you one. They've seen millions of friendly emails, so they can draft one for you. They've seen tens of thousands of cold sales pitches, so they can help you write a better one.

Think of it this way: imagine the most well-read intern in the world. They've read every article ever written, every recipe, every legal disclaimer, every grandma's letter. They can produce a draft of almost anything you ask for in seconds. But -- and this is important -- they don't actually understand what they're saying. They just pattern-match very well.

That's ChatGPT. That's Claude. That's all of them.

Why they sometimes "lie" (and how to spot it)

Because these tools are predicting words and not actually thinking, they sometimes produce something we call a hallucination.

A hallucination is when ChatGPT or Claude says something with total confidence that is completely wrong. They're not lying on purpose -- they don't know they're wrong. They're just predicting words that sound right based on patterns, and sometimes the pattern produces nonsense.

Examples of hallucinations you might see:

  • Fake quotes: ChatGPT might attribute a quote to someone who never said it.
  • Fake statistics: "47% of small businesses fail in their first year" -- but the actual number is something different, and the source ChatGPT cited doesn't exist.
  • Made-up sources: It might say "according to a 2023 Harvard Business Review article..." -- and the article doesn't exist.
  • Wrong details about real things: It might confuse two similarly-named people, or get a date wrong.

How to spot a hallucination

Three rules. Memorize these.

  1. If the AI cites a source -- verify it. Open a new tab, search for the article it referenced. If you can't find it, the source is probably made up.

  2. If the AI gives you a specific number, statistic, or date -- verify it. AI is bad at math and dates. Anything specific should be double-checked.

  3. If the AI sounds extremely confident about something obscure -- be more skeptical, not less. Confidence isn't proof. AI is just as confident when it's wrong as when it's right.


This is the single most important habit in the whole course. Master this and your work for clients will be polished and trustworthy from day one. Skip it, and you'll embarrass yourself eventually. Don't skip it.

Which tool is best for what?

Both ChatGPT and Claude are excellent. They have slightly different personalities and slightly different strengths. Here's how I think about it:

Use ChatGPT when you want:

  • Quick brainstorming -- "Give me 20 names for a baking blog"
  • Fast drafts of casual writing -- short emails, social media posts
  • Image generation (ChatGPT can make images; Claude generally can't)
  • Tools and integrations with other apps

Use Claude when you want:

  • Long-form writing -- articles, sales pages, course outlines
  • Anything that needs a thoughtful, careful tone -- client emails about delicate topics
  • Larger documents -- Claude can handle bigger inputs and outputs in one go
  • Better-quality default writing -- most people find Claude's first draft is more polished

The one rule that changes everything

Ready for the most important sentence in this entire course?

Always verify. Never copy-paste raw AI output to a client. Read every word, fact-check anything specific, and always make a small human edit before delivery.

Memorize that. Tape it to your monitor. Tattoo it on your forearm.

Here's why it matters:

Your clients are paying you for your judgment, not the AI's. If you submit raw AI output, you're delivering exactly what your client could have done themselves for $20/month. They'd fire you within a month, and they'd be right to.

What clients pay you for is the part the AI can't do:

  • Knowing which facts to keep and which to verify
  • Editing the tone to match their voice
  • Spotting when something sounds confidently wrong
  • Adding the small human touches that make writing feel like a real person, not a robot

That's your job. AI is just the assistant doing the heavy typing.

When you understand this, the whole career makes sense. You're not competing with AI. You're using it. That's the mindset shift.

Try it yourself: 10-minute exercise

Let's put this into practice. Total time: 10 minutes.

Step 1: Pick a real topic you know about

Something where you'd notice if an AI got the details wrong. Some ideas:

  • A hobby you've done for years (gardening, woodworking, baking, knitting, etc.)
  • Your previous career (accounting, teaching, healthcare, sales, etc.)
  • A topic you read about often (a sports team, a TV show, a historical era)

Step 2: Open ChatGPT and ask it a specific question

Open chat.openai.com in one tab. Ask something like:

  • Tell me three little-known facts about [your topic].
  • What's the most common mistake beginners make with [your topic]?
  • Who is considered the leading expert on [your topic]?

Press Enter and read the answer carefully.

Step 3: Spot a hallucination (or confirm it's all true)

Ask yourself:

  • Are the facts it gave you actually true? (Use your existing knowledge to check.)
  • Did it cite anyone or any source? If so, can you find that source by searching Google?
  • Did it sound confident about something that turned out to be wrong, vague, or made up?

Most likely: at least one thing it said will be slightly off, exaggerated, or completely fabricated. That's the hallucination. You just learned to spot one.

Step 4: Try the same prompt in Claude

Open claude.ai in another tab. Paste the exact same question.

Compare the two answers. Notice:

  • Was Claude more careful, or more confident?
  • Did Claude get the same facts right and wrong?
  • Which answer would you trust more for a real client?

You'll start to notice a pattern. Claude is generally a bit more cautious. ChatGPT is generally a bit more confident. Both are useful -- for different things.

If you got stuck (and remember -- this is normal)

The whole point of this course is to walk you through every single step, no judgment. If something didn't make sense, here are the most common spots people get tripped up:

"I don't know what topic to use for the exercise"

Use gardening. Or the weather. Or the history of your hometown. Anything you have personal knowledge about works. The goal is to spot when the AI is making something up -- you can only do that if you know enough about the topic to catch the error.

"Both ChatGPT and Claude gave me the same correct answer. Did I do something wrong?"

No, you did fine. Sometimes both tools have read the same accurate sources and they both get it right. Try a more obscure topic -- something you doubt the AI has much information about. Hallucinations are most common when you ask about niche or specific things.

"I don't really understand what 'predicting the next word' means"

That's okay. Here's an even simpler way to think about it:

ChatGPT and Claude are like the world's best autocomplete. When you type "How do I bake a...", they predict the next word is probably "cake." Then they predict the word after that. Then the word after that. Soon you have a full recipe. They don't know what cake is. They just know which words come next.

That's the whole concept. You don't need to understand it more deeply than that.

"The AI made up a source. Should I email and complain?"

You don't need to. Hallucinations are a known limitation of all current AI tools. OpenAI and Anthropic both publish warnings about them. The fix is on the user side -- you, verifying facts. Not on the company side.
