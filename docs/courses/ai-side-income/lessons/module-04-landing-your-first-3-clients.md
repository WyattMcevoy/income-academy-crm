## The 5 places to find your first clients

In rough order of "easiest to start" to "highest quality leads":

## Place 1: Upwork

### What it is

Upwork.com is where small businesses and entrepreneurs post jobs and hire freelancers. There are tens of thousands of jobs posted every week. Many are perfect for someone new to the field.

### Why it works for beginners

- Clients are actively looking to hire (vs. you cold-pitching them)
- The "Talent Marketplace" tier is open to everyone (no application required)
- You can land your first client without ever having had a portfolio

### Setup walkthrough

**1. Go to upwork.com and click "Sign Up as Freelancer."**

**2. Fill out the basics:** name, email, password.

**3. Choose your category.** Pick "Writing" if you chose Service #1, "Admin Support" if you chose #2 or #4, "Sales & Marketing" if you chose #3, "Translation" if you chose #5.

**4. Write your profile title.** Format: "[Service] for [Audience]." Examples:

- "Blog Writer for Small Local Businesses"
- "Bookkeeping Assistant for Etsy Sellers"
- "LinkedIn Ghostwriter for Realtors and Consultants"

**5. Write your profile overview** (300 to 400 words). Use this template:

```
I help [type of client] with [service] using a careful, hands-on
approach that prioritizes quality over volume.

What I do:
- [Specific deliverable 1]
- [Specific deliverable 2]
- [Specific deliverable 3]

What makes me different:
- I'm a [your past career or relevant background]
- I take the time to actually read what you send me
- I deliver on time, every time, and respond to messages within
  24 hours

Recent work I'm proud of: [If you don't have any, skip this section
for now. We'll add it after your first project.]

If you'd like to talk, send me a message. I respond within a day.
```

**6. Set your hourly rate.** Start at the lower end of the ranges from Module 3. You can raise rates after your first 5 reviews.

**7. Add a profile photo.** Use a real photo of yourself. Smile. Look directly at the camera. Phone selfie is fine if the lighting is good.

**8. Submit for approval.** Upwork reviews new profiles. You'll get approved within 24 to 48 hours.

### How to bid

Once your profile is live:

**1. Go to the "Find Work" tab.**

**2. Filter for jobs that match your service:**

- For writing: keyword "blog post" or "newsletter"
- For bookkeeping support: "bookkeeping assistant" or "data entry"
- For LinkedIn ghostwriting: "LinkedIn writer" or "social media ghostwriter"
- For email triage: "customer service" or "email support"
- For transcription: "transcription" or "podcast editor"

**3. Read each job carefully.** Skip jobs where:

- The budget is under $25 (race to the bottom)
- The client has zero reviews and zero history
- The post is poorly written or vague (suggests a difficult client)

**4. Write a bid** (called a "proposal" on Upwork) for jobs that look good:

```
Hi [client name],

Quick note - I noticed you mentioned [specific detail from their post].
That's exactly the kind of project I'd be a good fit for.

Here's how I'd approach this:

1. [Step 1 specific to their job]
2. [Step 2 specific to their job]
3. [Step 3 specific to their job]

I can have a first draft to you within [realistic time frame].

Pricing: [your rate, clearly stated].

If this looks like a fit, just send me a message and I'll send over
a few quick questions to make sure we're aligned.

[Your name]
```

**5. Aim for 2 to 3 bids per day** in your first 30 days. **Less than that and you won't land clients fast enough.** More than that and quality suffers.

### Realistic expectations

- First bid: probably won't get hired
- First 10 bids: maybe 1 reply
- First 25 bids: probably 1 hire
- After 50 bids: you'll have a feel for what works

This is normal. Every freelancer starts here.

## Place 2: Your existing network

### What it is

People who already know you, trust you, and would happily refer you - if they only knew what you now offer.

### Why it works for our age group

You've spent decades building relationships. **You probably know 100 to 500 people** between former coworkers, neighbors, friends from church or community groups, family members, parents of your kids' friends, and so on.

Younger freelancers don't have this. **It's your unfair advantage.**

### The "I'm trying something new" email

Send this to 10 to 20 people in your existing network this week. Don't ask them to hire you. Just let them know what you're up to.

```
Subject: A quick update - I'd love your thoughts

Hi [Name],

Hope you're doing well. Life's been good - [one specific update
about you, like grandkids, recent trip, etc.].

I wanted to share something I'm trying out and ask for your honest
take.

Over the past couple months I've been learning how to use AI tools
(ChatGPT and Claude) to help small businesses with [your specific
service - e.g., "writing newsletters and blog posts"]. I'm taking
on a few clients to get started.

I'm not selling anything to you - I just thought you might know
someone who's been wishing they had help with this kind of thing.
Realtors, contractors, consultants, small online sellers, that
sort of person.

If anyone comes to mind, would you mind passing along my name?

Either way, would love to hear how you're doing. Coffee soon?

[Your name]
```

### Why this works

- It's not pushy
- It's specific (so people can actually picture who to refer)
- It opens with caring about them, not selling
- The "either way" phrasing removes pressure

### Realistic expectations

Out of 20 emails like this, expect:

- 12 to 15 friendly replies
- 2 to 4 referrals (someone they know who might be interested)
- 1 to 2 actual conversations with potential clients
- 0 to 1 paying client in the first 30 days

That's a great result. **Referrals from your network become your steadiest clients long-term.**

## Place 3: Local small business outreach

### What it is

Find 20 small businesses in your town in a specific niche. Send each one a personalized email. Get 1 or 2 responses. Have a conversation. Sometimes get hired.

### The "20 emails" technique

**1. Pick a niche.** Some good ones:

- Chiropractors
- Dentists (general practice)
- Independent insurance agents
- Pest control companies
- Local accountants
- Independent plumbers / HVAC / electricians
- Realtors at small-to-mid size brokerages

**2. Open Google.** Search: "[niche] near [your town]"

**3. Find 20 local businesses.** Open each one's website. Note:

- The owner's first name (usually on the "About" page)
- One specific thing about their business (a unique service, a quote on their site, an article they wrote)
- Their email address (usually "info@" or the owner's name@ on the contact page)

**4. Write a personalized email to each one** using this template:

```
Subject: [Owner's first name] - quick question about [their business]

Hi [first name],

I came across [their business name] when I was looking around for
[chiropractors/insurance/etc.] in [town]. I noticed [one specific
thing you noticed - their tagline, an article they wrote, a service
they offer that's unusual].

I help local [niche] businesses with [your service] using AI as my
behind-the-scenes assistant. Recent example: [one-sentence example,
or "still building my portfolio - looking to take on 2-3 starter
clients at a discounted rate"].

If you've ever thought "I should be doing more [content / email
follow-ups / blog posts / etc.] but I just don't have the time,"
I'd love to chat for 15 minutes.

Either way, hope you have a good week.

[Your name]
```

**5. Send them out over a week.** 4 a day for 5 days. Don't blast all 20 at once - that looks like spam.

**6. Track replies in a simple Google Sheet.**

### Realistic expectations

Out of 20 emails:

- 8 to 12 will be ignored (normal)
- 4 to 6 will reply politely saying "no thanks" (normal)
- 2 to 4 will reply asking to learn more (great!)
- 1 to 2 will become real conversations
- 0 to 1 will become a paying client in the first 30 days

If you do this for 3 weeks straight, you should have a paying client.

## Place 4: Facebook groups for small business owners

### What it is

Join 3 to 5 active Facebook groups where small business owners hang out. Spend 2 weeks just being helpful (not selling). Then mention what you do once.

### Good groups to look for

- "[Your city] Small Business Owners"
- "Solopreneur Network"
- "Etsy Sellers Helping Etsy Sellers"
- "Realtors Helping Realtors"
- Any group titled "[Niche] Small Business" with 5,000+ members and recent posts

### The 2-week patience play

**Week 1:**

- Join 3 to 5 groups
- Read posts every day
- Comment helpfully on 2 to 3 posts per day - real, useful answers, no plug for your services
- Get to know who the active members are

**Week 2:**

- Continue commenting helpfully
- Start a post: "Honest question for the group - what's the [content / customer service / paperwork] task you keep putting off because you don't have time? Curious what's most painful right now."
- Read the responses. Reply thoughtfully to each.

**Week 3:**

- People will start asking you what you do. **That's when you get to mention it.**
- Reply with: "I help with exactly that. Happy to chat if you want - send me a message."
- Take conversations to private message, then to a 15-minute call.

### Why it works

- You're not cold-pitching - you've been helpful for weeks first
- People recognize your name by week 3
- The opportunity to mention what you do is *invited*, not pushed
- Group members refer each other constantly

### Realistic expectations

After 4 weeks of doing this consistently in 3 to 5 groups:

- 1 to 2 client conversations per week
- 1 paying client by end of week 6 to 8

Slower than other methods, but the clients you get this way are loyal and refer others.

## Place 5: Other professionals' clients (the gold mine)

### What it is

Accountants, lawyers, designers, and consultants regularly hear their clients say things like "I should be doing more marketing" or "I'm drowning in admin work." If they had someone trustworthy to refer those clients to, **they would.**

That's where you come in.

### How to do it

**1. Pick 5 local professionals to reach out to:**

- 2 small accounting firms
- 1 attorney who does small-business law
- 1 graphic designer or web developer
- 1 marketing consultant

**2. Email each one with this:**

```
Subject: A quick referral idea, [first name]

Hi [first name],

I came across your firm when I was looking for [accountants /
attorneys / etc.] in [town]. Your work caught my eye because
[specific thing about their site or LinkedIn].

I'm a [your service] specialist - I help small business owners
with [specific thing]. The thing is, I keep talking to clients
who really need [accounting / legal / design] help that I can't
provide. I imagine you have the reverse experience too - clients
who need [your service] but it's outside what you do.

Want to grab a 15-minute coffee (or Zoom) to see if we can refer
each other when those situations come up?

Either way, glad your work is doing well.

[Your name]
```

**3. Take the meeting.** Be helpful. Don't be salesy.

**4. After the meeting, send a follow-up:**

```
Hi [first name],

Great chatting today. I'll keep you in mind anytime I hear a
client mention [accounting / legal / etc.]. If you ever hear
someone mention [your service], I'd be honored if you sent them
my way.

Here's a one-paragraph description you can copy and paste into
an email if it's ever helpful:

"[Your name] helps small businesses with [service]. They use
AI tools to deliver fast, high-quality work without the overhead
of an agency. Their rates are reasonable and they care a lot
about their clients."

Thanks again,
[Your name]
```

### Why this works

These professionals are referral machines. Once they refer you ONE good client and that client has a good experience, you'll get more.

### Realistic expectations

Out of 5 emails to professionals:

- 3 will reply
- 2 will take the meeting
- 1 will become a regular referral source within 6 months
- That 1 referral source might send you 5 to 10 clients per year

## If you got stuck

### "I'm too nervous to send my first email"

Most people are. Some tricks that help:

- Send to your LEAST important prospect first - someone where rejection wouldn't bother you
- Have a friend read your draft and give you a thumbs up
- Tell yourself you're going to send 5 today and only count after the 5th one
- Remember: the worst that happens is they don't reply. **You won't be embarrassed - you won't even hear back. That's it.**

### "I sent 10 emails and no one replied"

Normal. You haven't been ignored - you've just learned what doesn't work yet.

Try one of these tweaks:

- Make your subject line more curious ("Quick question about [their business]" works better than "Hi from a freelancer")
- Make your opening line specifically about THEM (not about you)
- Shorten the email by 30% - long emails get skipped
- Try a different niche

### "I got a reply but I don't know what to say next"

The next move is always: **suggest a 15-minute call** to learn more about their situation. Module 5 covers exactly what to say on that call.
