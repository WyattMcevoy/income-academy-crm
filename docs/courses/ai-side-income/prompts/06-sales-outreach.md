# Prompts — Sales & Outreach

For finding clients through cold email, Upwork proposals, and follow-ups.

## Cold email

### SO-01: Cold email first draft
```
Write a cold email from me to [PROSPECT BUSINESS NAME]. 

My research on them:
- What they do: [BRIEF]
- Something specific I noticed: [ANYTHING FROM WEBSITE, LINKEDIN, NEWS]
- Likely pain point I can address: [GUESS]

My service: [YOUR SERVICE, BRIEFLY]
One specific example of past work or credibility: [BRIEF]

Rules:
- Under 120 words
- First sentence references something specific to THEM
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- End with ONE specific ask (15-min call, not a vague "let's connect")
- Professional but human — no corporate speak
```

### SO-02: Personalize a template for a specific prospect
```
Here's my cold email template:
[PASTE TEMPLATE]

The prospect:
- Business: [NAME + TYPE]
- Their website headline: [PASTE]
- Their recent LinkedIn post or announcement: [PASTE]
- My best guess at their current priority: [GUESS]

Customize the template for this specific prospect. Replace every [BRACKET]. Keep the word count under 120.
```

### SO-03: Follow-up (no response after 4-5 days)
```
I sent this email to [PROSPECT] on [DATE]:
[PASTE ORIGINAL EMAIL]

They haven't responded. Write a follow-up that:
- Under 60 words
- Doesn't apologize or say "bumping this up"
- Adds ONE new value (a thought, resource, or observation) not in the first email
- Soft close, doesn't beg

Subject line: "re: [SAME SUBJECT]"
```

### SO-04: Final follow-up (closing the loop)
```
I've sent 2 emails to [PROSPECT] with no response. Write a polite closing-the-loop email:
- Under 50 words
- Genuine, not passive-aggressive
- Leaves door open without begging
- No guilt trip

Example voice: "understanding you're busy, I'll stop cluttering the inbox. If [TOPIC] ever becomes relevant, feel free to reach back. All the best."
```

## Upwork proposals

### SO-05: Upwork proposal from job description
```
Job description:
[PASTE]

Client's budget: [AMOUNT]
Hourly or fixed: [WHICH]

Write a proposal that:
1. First sentence shows I READ the job (not generic "I'm interested")
2. Addresses their specific need with ONE concrete example of how I'd approach it
3. Asks ONE clarifying question that shows expertise
4. States my rate/bid
5. Ends with a specific next step

Max 200 words. Tone: confident but not cocky. Professional but human.
```

### SO-06: Upwork profile headline brainstorm
```
My services: [LIST]
My experience: [BRIEF]
My target client: [WHO]

Generate 10 Upwork profile headline options that:
- Are specific (not "Freelance Writer" — too generic)
- Mention the client type I want (e.g., "for small business owners")
- Under 120 characters
- Contain at least one specific skill or niche

Rank the top 3.
```

### SO-07: Upwork profile rewrite for my strengths
```
Here's my current Upwork profile overview:
[PASTE]

Rewrite it so it:
- Opens with what I do FOR CLIENTS (not what I am)
- Lists 3-5 specific skills with ONE example of each
- Includes a sentence about my client type
- Closes with a soft CTA
- Under 400 words
- Removes filler like "passionate," "detail-oriented," "hardworking" — show, don't claim
```

## Discovery call prep

### SO-08: Pre-call research brief
```
Tomorrow I have a discovery call with [PROSPECT NAME] from [COMPANY].

What I know:
[PASTE LINKEDIN, WEBSITE, ANY CONTEXT]

Generate a 1-page pre-call brief:
- Company in 2 sentences
- Their likely priorities this quarter
- 3 questions I should ask
- 2 things to avoid saying (potential landmines)
- My best guess at what they'll offer / their budget range
- How I should position my service given their stage

Length: fits on one screen.
```

### SO-09: Discovery call talking points
```
Given this brief:
[PASTE BRIEF FROM SO-08]

Generate my opening (first 2 minutes of the call):
- Genuine, warm greeting
- Agenda setter (what I plan to cover in 30 min)
- One question that invites them to talk about their situation

Write it like I'll say it out loud. Short sentences. Natural pauses.
```

## Post-call follow-up

### SO-10: Post-call summary email
```
I just had a discovery call with [PROSPECT]. 

Key points discussed:
- Their main problem: [BRIEF]
- Their goal: [BRIEF]
- What they've tried: [BRIEF]
- Budget range (if mentioned): [AMOUNT]
- Timeline: [WHEN]
- Next step we agreed to: [WHAT + WHEN]

Write the follow-up email:
- Thanks them for the call
- Recaps what I heard (2-3 bullets — confirm understanding)
- Restates the next step + timeline
- Professional sign-off

Length: 200 words max. Tone: confident, organized.
```

### SO-11: Proposal delivery email
```
I'm sending [PROSPECT] a proposal based on our discovery call.

Key pieces of the proposal:
- Scope: [BRIEF]
- Price: $[AMOUNT]
- Timeline: [DURATION]
- Delivery date: [WHEN]

Write the email that accompanies the proposal attachment:
- Reference the specific call (not generic)
- Highlight ONE thing in the proposal I'm especially proud of or think they'll like
- Give a clear next step ("sign and return by [DATE]" or "call me at [TIME] to discuss")
- Under 150 words
```

## Handling objections / negotiations

### SO-12: Price objection response
```
The prospect replied: "That's more than I expected."

Context:
- I quoted $[AMOUNT]
- The scope covers [BRIEF]
- Their situation: [WHAT I KNOW ABOUT THEIR BUDGET]

Write 2 response options:
- A: stand firm, explain why (without apologizing)
- B: offer a smaller version for lower price (reduce scope, maintain rate-per-value)

Both options: professional, not defensive. Under 120 words each.
```

### SO-13: "We want to go with someone cheaper" response
```
Prospect says they've decided to hire someone with a lower quote.

Write a response that:
- Is gracious, not bitter
- Wishes them well briefly
- Leaves the door open ("if it doesn't work out, I'm here")
- Under 60 words
- Doesn't beg or try to salvage

Sometimes cheaper is right for them. Don't argue.
```

### SO-14: "Can you do it faster?" response
```
Prospect wants delivery [N] days faster than my quoted timeline.

Context:
- Original timeline: [WEEKS]
- They want: [FASTER TIMELINE]
- My actual capacity to compress: [HONEST — can I really do it?]

Write a response:
- If I can compress with a rush fee: quote the fee + explain why
- If I can't compress: explain briefly, suggest prioritizing parts
- Never say "yes" just to win the deal if I can't actually deliver

Under 120 words. Tone: honest, practical.
```

## Referral / network outreach

### SO-15: "I just launched" announcement to network
```
I'm launching my freelance practice offering [SERVICE] to [TARGET CLIENT TYPE].

Write an email to send to my personal/professional network (friends, former colleagues) announcing the shift:
- Brief (under 200 words)
- Explains the change without being salesy
- Makes it easy for them to refer people (ideal client description, how to contact me)
- Tone: confident, not apologetic
- Not a formal newsletter — personal

Include: one specific example of the type of client/project I'm looking for.
```

### SO-16: Referral thank-you
```
Someone in my network referred a prospect to me. Write a thank-you email:
- Short (under 80 words)
- Genuine, specific to the referral
- Updates them on the outcome (if appropriate)
- Offers something back if natural (refer them work, small gift, whatever fits)
- Doesn't make them feel like they need to do more
```

## LinkedIn outreach (if using)

### SO-17: LinkedIn connection request note
```
I want to connect with [PROSPECT] on LinkedIn. 

Their profile summary:
[BRIEF]

Write a connection note that:
- Under 300 characters (LinkedIn limit)
- References something specific from their profile
- Gives a clear, non-salesy reason I'm connecting
- Doesn't pitch anything

Example voice: "Saw your post on [X] — really resonated. Always glad to connect with [their role/industry] doing good work. [First name]"
```

### SO-18: LinkedIn DM sequence (after they accept)
```
[PROSPECT] accepted my LinkedIn connection.

Write the first follow-up DM:
- Under 150 words
- Reference something specific that made me want to connect
- Ask ONE curious question about their work
- Don't pitch. Don't link. Don't sell.
- Goal is to build a real connection — pitch comes (if at all) much later

Then write the second DM (after their reply):
- Engage with what they said specifically
- Natural next step (another question, share a relevant resource, suggest a call if natural)
```

---

## Outreach volume guidance

- 5-15 cold outreach sends per day is sustainable
- Batch research + batch writing (10 at once, not one-off)
- Track in a simple spreadsheet
- Expect 3-5% reply rate for cold emails (if quality is high)
- Expect 20-40% reply rate for warm/referred intros
- Don't scale broken messaging — find what works first, then volume

## Psychological rule

The best outreach emails show the prospect you care more about understanding their world than pitching your service. The pitch is actually subtle — they almost feel like they're the one proposing the call because you asked the right questions.
